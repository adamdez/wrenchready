The Real Schedule and What It Means

What this schedule enables
Simon’s production capacity
Weekday evenings (4–9 PM): 2 jobs per evening is realistic. 5 hrs allows for 1 parts pickup + 2 jobs with buffers. Most residential customers are HOME during this window, which is ideal for mobile.
Saturday (7 AM–7 PM): 5–6 jobs. This is Simon’s big production day. 12 hours allows full route with proper breaks.
Weekly peak capacity: 10 weekday jobs + 5–6 Saturday jobs = 15–16 jobs/week.
Weekly realistic average (once ramped): 8–10 weekday jobs + 4–5 Saturday jobs = 12–15 jobs/week.
At $175–$240 blended ARO: 12–15 jobs/week = $2,100–$3,600/week = $9K–$15K/month.
Hard truth: a single mechanic working evenings + Saturdays tops out around $12K–$16K/month at peak. To hit $22K–$30K/month, Simon either needs to go full-time (8+ hours/day on weekdays) or you need a second technician. The plan is built around what this schedule CAN do, and flags when you need to decide about going full-time.
Dez’s capacity — 70 hrs/week is massive
Morning block (7–12): Systems build, marketing, fleet outreach, content, ads management, parts research and pre-ordering.
Afternoon block (12–4): Outreach visits (tow companies, parts counters, body shops, fleet prospects). Follow-up calls. Deferred-work texts. Bookkeeping.
Evening block (4–9): LIVE SUPPORT while Simon is wrenching. Answer calls, send estimates, send review requests within 10 min of job completion, handle customer communication, log findings, build next-day schedule.
This evening overlap is the key advantage: Dez is running real-time admin support while Simon wrenches. No delays on estimates, reviews, or follow-up.
Sunday: Dez uses this for weekly KPI review, content creation, ad optimization, fleet prep, and system improvements. No customer-facing work on Sunday.

R&D Metrics: What This Launch Must Prove
This launch is not just building a local business. It is collecting the data and proving the systems that make multi-market replication possible. Every KPI below serves dual purposes: near-term revenue AND long-term model validation.
Transferability Metrics (Track from Month 1)
DVI completion rate: must reach 100% on every maintenance visit. If this only works when Simon does it, the model does not transfer.
Estimate turnaround without founder: once AI drafting is active, measure how fast estimates go out when Simon is NOT involved. Target: <15 minutes from diagnosis to customer receipt.
Documentation completeness: % of jobs with full records in the system of record (photos, findings, approvals, invoice, deferred work). Target: 100%. If this requires Simon’s personal discipline to maintain, it is a founder hack, not a system.
Review velocity: % of completed jobs that produce a Google review. Target: 25%+. This must work through automation, not through Simon’s personal charm.
Deferred-work conversion rate: % of SOON items that convert to booked work within 90 days. Target: 25–40%. This proves the follow-up system works.
Repeat booking rate: % of customers who return within 12 months. Target: 35–50%. This proves the reminder system works.
Route efficiency: average drive time between jobs. Target: <20 min. This proves zone discipline and scheduling quality.
Coordinator trainability: when Dez documents a workflow, can another person execute it within 1 day of training? Test this by Month 6.
Founder Hacks vs. Permanent System Design
Some launch decisions are temporary shortcuts that work because the founders are doing everything. These must be identified and replaced with system solutions before replication.
The August 1 quarterly review should include an honest assessment: which of these founder hacks are still in place? Every one that remains is a barrier to hiring the first non-founder technician and to replicating in a second market.
