
WHY YOU FAILED
An Adversarial Pre-Mortem
for the Mobile Auto Repair Business
Seven expert agents tear apart the Investor Plan
and show you every way this company can die.

Spokane County, WA  •  March 2026  •  Version 3 • Final Board Edition

The purpose of this document is not to discourage. It is to prepare.
If you can survive this review, you can survive the market.

How to Use This Document
This pre-mortem assumes the business has already failed and works backward to identify the most plausible causes of death. It is written as a companion to the Investor Plan, not as a duplicate of it. The Investor Plan explains how the company is supposed to work. This document explains how it breaks.
Version 3 expands the original five-agent review into a broader nine-agent red-team analysis. It now covers finance, mechanical feasibility, marketing and demand, operations and scaling, legal exposure, customer trust and reputation, systems and documentation integrity, multi-market scale risk, and control tower and hiring risk — the areas that determine whether this company becomes a regional platform or stays a good local business.
Read this document in three passes. First, read each agent’s attacks straight through. Second, mark every attack that still does not have a concrete countermeasure in your actual operating system. Third, use the synthesis tables at the end as your weekly red-team checklist.
This is not a morale document. It is a failure-prevention instrument.
The adversarial panel


Agent 1: Startup Finance
“I’ve reviewed hundreds of first-year projections. This plan is more serious than most, but it still has several ways to run out of cash while looking profitable on paper.”
Attack 1: The breakout case is still back-loaded and fragile
A large share of the year-one upside still depends on late-year momentum. That means the business can look healthy in theory and still miss the annual number if month 10 or month 11 gets hit by injury, van failure, weather, or review damage.
VERDICT: A business that needs a perfect fourth quarter to look credible is not a robust first-year model.
FIX: Carry a base-case narrative that remains attractive even if the breakout case slips. Maintain business-interruption and vehicle contingency plans, and model at least one lost-month scenario in cash terms, not just revenue terms. (See Investor Plan Sections 3, 24.)
Attack 2: Cash flow timing can kill a profitable P&L
Insurance, tools, vehicle issues, startup parts inventory, software, and marketing all hit before revenue compounds. Fleet billing can also convert profitable work into slow cash. A year-end profit table does not protect you from month-two cash suffocation.
VERDICT: The business dies when bills come due before the appointment engine is mature enough to fund them.
FIX: Manage monthly cash — not annual profit — as the primary survival metric. Track lowest expected cash point, hold a 20% working-capital buffer above that, and do not confuse booked work with collected cash. (See Investor Plan Section 24: Cash Flow Forecast.)
Attack 3: Parts-margin compression is not a theory; it is daily customer behavior
Customers can instantly compare battery, alternator, and starter prices from their phones in the driveway. If your model quietly depends on aggressive markup across visible components, margin leaks every day even when jobs are closing.
VERDICT: The business becomes fragile if too much gross profit comes from customer ignorance instead of pricing discipline.
FIX: Underwrite the model at a lower blended markup, protect labor pricing, and treat visible parts categories differently from low-visibility consumables, kits, fluids, and incidental hardware. (See Investor Plan Section 6.8, Rule 9: Variable Markup by Price Visibility.)
Attack 4: Net-30 and parts float can turn growth into a financing problem
Fleet looks attractive because it smooths volume, but it can quietly make you the bank. You buy parts today, do the work now, and collect later — if the customer pays on time.
VERDICT: Top-line growth is not healthy if working capital is being consumed faster than it is replenished.
FIX: Set fleet credit rules, deposits for parts-heavy work, invoice aging triggers, and a clear collections process before chasing more commercial accounts. (See Investor Plan Section 12: Fleet Contract Strategy.)
Attack 5: The owner’s true economic reality may disappoint after the adrenaline wears off
The plan can produce strong owner income, but year one may still require 50–60 hour weeks, physical risk, no PTO, no guaranteed benefits, and concentrated dependence on one human body and one service vehicle.
VERDICT: If the owner’s effective hourly return and life quality do not feel meaningfully better than employment, enthusiasm collapses and discipline usually collapses with it.
FIX: Keep a brutally honest owner-economics section: income, hours, risk, and what the asset becomes if the operator survives long enough to systematize it. (See Investor Plan Section 15: P&L and Sensitivity Analysis.)


Agent 2: 30-Year Master Mechanic
“The business strategy is much sharper now, but driveway reality still punishes people who let clean spreadsheets outrun greasy reality.”
Attack 1: Spokane corrosion and age-related condition variability still wreck clean labor assumptions
A mobile time multiplier is helpful, but older winter-driven vehicles will still turn brake, suspension, cooling, and exhaust-adjacent work into messy, schedule-destroying jobs.
VERDICT: Under-car work on older vehicles is not just slower. It is less predictable, more tool-dependent, and more likely to produce broken bolts, stripped hardware, and same-day parts runs.
FIX: Price older vehicles more defensively, screen vehicle age and condition during intake, and maintain a hard refusal line for rust fights that do not fit the model. (See Investor Plan Section 8.1: Labor Time Multiplier.)
Attack 2: Diagnostics are only great margins when they are scoped correctly
A $150 diagnostic is not a magic ticket. Poorly screened no-start, overheating, drivability, or check-engine appointments can consume an hour and still end in a refer-out, a customer complaint, or no immediate revenue expansion.
VERDICT: Diagnostic work is only high-margin when the intake is strong, the service lane is bounded, and the customer leaves feeling they received clarity rather than a shrug.
FIX: Use symptom-based triage before booking, define what your mobile diagnostic lane covers, and frame the diagnostic as a decision service with documented findings, not a guarantee of same-day resolution. (See Investor Plan Section 7: Diagnostic-First Workflow.)
Attack 3: Brake work remains the highest-volume mechanical profit lane and the highest comeback risk
One rushed brake job can undo ten clean ones. Torque errors, unlubricated slides, bent shims, contaminated pads, weak test drives, or poor bedding procedure can all become expensive trust failures after you leave the site.
VERDICT: Brake work is not dangerous because it is hard. It is dangerous because it is common enough to make corners feel acceptable.
FIX: Keep brake work protocolized: inspection before quoting, torque verification, bedding procedure, recheck discipline, and a customer follow-up loop that catches problems before a review does. (See Investor Plan Section 26.3: Brake Comeback Prevention Protocol.)
Attack 4: Scope creep is still the mechanic’s daily enemy
What starts as one repair often reveals a second failure, a missing part, or a safety item that changes the economics and timing of the job. If you do not have an on-the-spot discovery protocol, schedule integrity disappears and trust usually follows.
VERDICT: Mid-job ambiguity is where profitability dies — either through unpaid labor, bad communication, or a parts run that blows the day apart.
FIX: Treat additional findings as a formal micro-sales and documentation event, not as improvised conversation. (See Investor Plan Section 26.4: Mid-Job Discovery Protocol.)
Attack 5: Limited test-drive conditions are a structural weakness, not a coaching issue
A mobile mechanic does not control the environment the way a shop does. That means some post-repair symptoms will only show up after the customer leaves, especially on drivability and higher-speed brake complaints.
VERDICT: Not every risk can be eliminated. Some must be acknowledged, priced, and managed through procedure.
FIX: Build verification time into estimates, define which repairs require short road verification, and be explicit in the operating system about what can and cannot be fully validated onsite.
Attack 6: Vehicle-class margin traps destroy profitability on truck and HD work
The plan now documents that F-250 brake pads cost 2–3× what CR-V pads cost, and the 7.3L V8 has 16 spark plugs vs. 4 on a compact SUV. But if the operator quotes from memory or uses flat “from $X” pricing without checking vehicle-class cost data, truck and HD work can quietly destroy gross margin on what feels like a high-ticket job.
VERDICT: A $550 brake job on an F-250 that costs you $320 in parts earns less GP/hr than a $380 brake job on a CR-V that costs you $120 in parts. The truck job FEELS more profitable because the ticket is bigger. It is not.
FIX: Use vehicle-class pricing on every estimate. Reference the real parts cost tables in the plan (Section 6.7) before quoting. Never assume a truck job is automatically more profitable than a car job.


Agent 3: Marketing & Demand Engine
“The channel list is respectable. The real question is whether the business can build a compounding appointment engine instead of a pile of one-off jobs.”
Attack 1: The business may get leads without getting a true demand engine
It is possible to stay busy and still fail because the calendar is filled by scattered, low-quality, low-retention jobs. Leads are not the same as a functioning appointment machine.
VERDICT: If inquiry volume does not convert into repeat maintenance, deferred-work conversions, reviews, and referrals, the model stays fragile and forever dependent on new-customer hustle.
FIX: Track the full funnel: lead, booked appointment, completed visit, same-visit add-on, deferred work created, deferred work converted, repeat booking, and referral generation. (See Investor Plan Section 10: Demand Engine and Appointment Math.)
Attack 2: Review velocity can still miss the trust threshold
Without review momentum, Google Business Profile performance lags, close rates lag, and paid acquisition gets more expensive. Early reputation density matters more than founders want to admit.
VERDICT: A good business with weak review mechanics still loses to a slightly worse business with stronger local proof.
FIX: Treat review capture like an operating protocol, not a nice-to-have. Trigger it at job close, follow up once, and monitor actual review conversion rate weekly. (See Investor Plan Section 27: Playbook 4.)
Attack 3: Fleet is powerful but too slow to rescue launch-stage weakness
Fleet contracts are excellent later. Early on, they are seeds, not oxygen. If retail demand, referrals, and repeat behavior are weak, fleet will not save month one, month two, or month three.
VERDICT: The worst version of the plan assumes future fleet stability before present retail proof exists.
FIX: Pursue fleet from day one, but do not underwrite early survival around it. (See Investor Plan Section 10.3: Where Customers Come From by Stage.)
Attack 4: The maintenance engine may fail to compound
Oil changes, inspections, battery tests, and routine maintenance only become a strategic advantage if they create future appointments. Otherwise they are just low-ticket volume with windshield time.
VERDICT: Maintenance is not valuable because it is common. It is valuable because it is supposed to create repeat behavior and higher-margin downstream work.
FIX: Measure maintenance revisit rate, attachment rate, deferred-work close rate, and the percentage of first-time customers who generate a second revenue event within 120 days. (See Investor Plan Section 9.1: Oil Change Monetization Model.)
Attack 5: Paid acquisition can still be a vanity trap
Google Ads or LSAs can create motion before they create economics. If close rates, review density, and ARO are weak, ad spend simply accelerates unprofitable traffic.
VERDICT: Paid growth is dangerous when the underlying business still leaks trust or conversion.
FIX: Do not scale paid channels until the business can show acceptable conversion, response-time discipline, and route-hour economics. (See Investor Plan Section 11.4.)
Attack 6: Spokane winter crushes demand, not just productivity
The plan addresses weather as a productivity issue (15–22 reduced days/year). But December through February is also a demand problem — customers defer maintenance, cancel appointments, and avoid having someone work in their snowy driveway. Revenue can drop 15–30% in the slowest months regardless of operator willingness to work.
VERDICT: Winter is not just harder to work in. It is harder to sell in. The demand engine weakens at the same time the schedule gets disrupted.
FIX: Build cash reserves during peak months (May–October). Run seasonal promotions (winterization packages, battery testing campaigns) that create urgency during the months customers are most worried about their vehicles. Schedule covered fleet work on the worst weather days. (See Investor Plan Section 8.6: Seasonal Revenue Fluctuation.)


Agent 4: Operations & Scaling
“The biggest threat is not that the plan lacks ambition. It is that the operator eventually becomes the bottleneck, the van becomes the single point of failure, and the second route arrives before the first is mature.”
Attack 1: Owner burnout is not a risk line item; it is an operating certainty
A solo mobile business piles mechanic work, phone work, parts work, estimating, content, review chasing, dispatch, and bookkeeping onto one person. That load becomes unmanageable long before the founder stops believing in the idea.
VERDICT: Without hard boundaries, the business slowly becomes a life-quality destroyer, and quality drifts with exhaustion.
FIX: Keep a mandatory day off, a hard stop time, and an early admin-relief trigger. Treat sustained 65+ hour weeks as a break-glass event, not as a badge of honor. The admin partner exists specifically to prevent this. (See Investor Plan Section 20: Guardrails, and Operating Philosophy.)
Attack 2: The van is your shop, warehouse, billboard, and route engine; when it fails, everything fails
One-van businesses are more fragile than they look. A breakdown does not just delay one job. It scrambles inventory, scheduling, customer confidence, and local review momentum.
VERDICT: Vehicle failure is an existential event when the business is concentrated in one rolling asset.
FIX: Create a backup plan before launch: rental path, temporary alternate vehicle, transfer-ready tool organization, and clear customer communication protocol for route disruption.
Attack 3: The first field hire is still the most dangerous scaling moment
One bad technician can reverse months of trust creation, create liability, and weaken the owner’s own route because now the owner is managing, fixing, and reviewing instead of just producing.
VERDICT: Scaling exposes weak supervision faster than it exposes strong recruiting.
FIX: Gate the first hire through ride-alongs, probation, photo audits, technician-only service lanes at first, and direct customer follow-up after early jobs. (See Investor Plan Section 26.1: First-Hire Controls.)
Attack 4: The second route can cannibalize the first
Many service businesses do not fail because the second route loses money directly. They fail because the first route weakens while the owner’s attention is fragmented across hiring, quality control, scheduling, and troubleshooting.
VERDICT: Added complexity is not the same as added capacity.
FIX: Do not add a second route until the first is systematized, the coordinator function is real, and the owner’s own production can absorb some management drag without collapsing. (See Investor Plan Section 25: Second-Route Breakeven Analysis.)
Attack 5: Calendar fullness can hide operational weakness
A packed schedule feels like success, but if jobs are low quality, too far apart, over-serviced, or mis-scoped, the business can be busy while effective hourly returns stagnate.
VERDICT: Busyness is one of the most dangerous false positives in field service.
FIX: Track gross profit per route hour, travel time, job-type mix, and comeback-adjusted profitability — not just jobs per day. (See Investor Plan Section 27: Playbook 7, Monthly Dashboard.)


Agent 5: Legal & Liability
“This business does not die only in court. It dies when weak legal habits turn routine field work into disputes you cannot document your way out of.”
Attack 1: Over-compliance must be designed, not assumed
Mobile work lives in the gray edges of laws written for shops. If the customer rights notice, estimate process, or authorization history is weak, you are one dispute away from learning how little a verbal explanation is worth.
VERDICT: When the law is slightly ambiguous, the business must behave as if the stricter interpretation will be used against it.
FIX: Use belt-and-suspenders compliance: visible rights notice, estimate language, customer initials where appropriate, and searchable authorization records. (See Investor Plan Section 5, Step 6: RCW 46.71 Compliance.)
Attack 2: Property-damage claims can become reputation events, not just insurance events
Driveways, garage floors, nearby vehicles, landscaping, and home-adjacent structures are all potential claim surfaces. Even when insured, these incidents cost time, trust, and future referrals.
VERDICT: A field business working on private property must behave like a guest and a risk manager at the same time.
FIX: Photograph the site, use mats and drip trays, mark a work zone, and document pre-existing conditions before tools come out. (See Investor Plan Section 26.2: Site Safety and Property Protection.)
Attack 3: Customer injury risk is easy to underestimate because it feels unlikely — until it happens
Children, pets, curious owners, neighbors, and shared parking environments create real hazard exposure. The legal problem is often not the injury itself; it is the lack of written safety habit preceding it.
VERDICT: If the site does not feel like controlled workspace, it becomes an uncontrolled liability surface.
FIX: Standardize work-zone rules, refuse customer assistance, and train every technician to protect the work area as aggressively as they protect the vehicle.
Attack 4: Environmental handling failures combine cleanup cost, regulatory exposure, and neighborhood trust damage
A spill in a customer’s driveway is not just a cleanup problem. It can become a county reporting issue, a homeowner conflict, and a future reputation drag if handled poorly or hidden.
VERDICT: Small field businesses often treat environmental discipline like a logistics detail until the first real spill forces it into the center of the business.
FIX: Maintain visible spill-readiness, site protection, and a documented response protocol that starts before the drain plug is touched. (See Investor Plan Section 26.5: Spill Response Protocol.)


Agent 6: Customer Trust & Reputation
“The plan now has a real ARO engine. That also means it now carries a new risk: sounding like the kind of operator people warn their friends about.”
Attack 1: Your inspection process can be perceived as sales theater
When every oil change turns into multiple recommendations, customers begin to feel that the inspection exists to manufacture revenue rather than surface truth. That perception is enough to depress reviews even when some recommendations are technically valid.
VERDICT: A trust engine becomes a reputation liability the moment customers feel the conclusion was preloaded before the inspection started.
FIX: Use evidence-first recommendations, limit same-visit asks to the top one to three relevant actions, and normalize the phrase “you do not have to do all of this today.” (See Investor Plan Section 9.3: Evidence-Based Recommendations and Section 9.7: Revenue Guardrails.)
Attack 2: Same-visit add-ons can quietly become pressure selling
Convenience pricing is smart. Convenience pressure is not. If the operator uses “while I’m here” language to make every decline feel foolish, customers will remember the emotional pressure more than the mechanical logic.
VERDICT: Short-term ticket size can destroy long-term customer value if the customer leaves feeling managed rather than helped.
FIX: Teach the difference between presenting options and steering outcomes. Offer real choice, not fake choice. (See Investor Plan Section 9.8: Customer Communication Scripts.)
Attack 3: One review that says ‘they always find something’ can poison your growth curve
A business built on trust, reviews, and local proof is highly exposed to a pattern of feedback that suggests unnecessary recommendations. Even if only a few customers feel that way, it can change how the next fifty customers hear your pitch.
VERDICT: Reputation risk compounds faster than most owner-operators realize because it affects both close rates and ad efficiency.
FIX: Audit recommendation frequency, same-visit close patterns, and review language. If customers start describing the business as pushy, fix the process immediately.
Attack 4: Deferred work can feel manipulative if it is not clearly prioritized
Logging recommendations for later is good practice. But if every customer leaves with a long list of future work, the pipeline can look less like thoughtful maintenance planning and more like manufactured backlog.
VERDICT: A deferred-work system without visible prioritization becomes credibility debt.
FIX: Use explicit Now / Soon / Monitor structure and tie each recommendation to evidence, interval, or observed condition. (See Investor Plan Section 9.4: Now / Soon / Monitor Framework.)
Attack 5: Revenue growth can outpace trust discipline
As the operator gets better at selling and the team grows, it becomes easier for the business to optimize ARO faster than it optimizes judgment. That is how companies drift into the exact reputation they said they would avoid.
VERDICT: The most dangerous version of success is profitable behavior that quietly corrodes the brand.
FIX: Create trust guardrails: no blanket flush selling, no unsupported urgency, no burying customers in ten findings, and no bonuses that reward ticket size without review quality. (See Investor Plan Section 9.7: Revenue Guardrails and Operating Philosophy.)


Agent 7: Systems, Documentation & Data Integrity
“This company can survive weak polish. It cannot survive fragmented records once jobs, deferred work, and multiple humans start moving through the same customer base.”
Attack 1: You may end up with three versions of the truth
If the owner, coordinator, technician, and customer messages all live in different places, disputes become hard to reconstruct and follow-up becomes unreliable. That is not just messy; it is expensive.
VERDICT: A service business fails operationally when no one can answer with confidence what was found, what was approved, what was declined, and what was promised.
FIX: Keep one system of record and make every estimate, inspection, photo, approval, invoice, and declined-work note flow back into it. (See Investor Plan Section 14: Documentation Architecture.)
Attack 2: Weak documentation turns routine disputes into unwinnable disputes
Without good records, a customer can honestly — or strategically — say they never approved the work, never saw the condition, never heard the warning, or were never told the job had changed.
VERDICT: Documentation is not admin. It is legal defense, warranty memory, and customer-trust infrastructure.
FIX: Document pre-existing site condition, findings, approvals, declines, photos, and follow-up in a searchable system every time. (See Investor Plan Sections 14.1 and 26.2.)
Attack 3: Deferred work without disciplined follow-up is fake asset value
A long list of declined recommendations looks impressive in theory. In practice, it is worthless unless the business can prioritize, revisit, and close it later.
VERDICT: Undeployed deferred work is not pipeline. It is clutter.
FIX: Track deferred work by urgency, vehicle, dollar value, and next contact date. Kill old clutter and focus on the items that are actually likely to convert. (See Investor Plan Section 9.5E: Deferred-Work Capture.)
Attack 4: Software sprawl can outgrow the business before the business outgrows the software
When texts, calendars, spreadsheets, invoices, photos, and reminders all live in different tools, coordination starts depending on memory and heroics. That works until one person takes a day off or one tech joins the system.
VERDICT: A business with fragmented tools scales confusion faster than revenue.
FIX: Choose one operational system of record early and keep custom layers thin and purposeful until the local model is truly proven. (See Investor Plan Section 14.5: Build vs. Buy Guardrails.)
Attack 5: Data inconsistency will eventually corrupt pricing, follow-up, and trust
If vehicle classes, job types, parts usage, decline reasons, and route zones are not recorded consistently, the business loses the ability to learn from its own operation. Then pricing gets sloppy, follow-up gets generic, and job screening weakens.
VERDICT: A business that cannot trust its own data eventually starts making emotional rather than operational decisions.
FIX: Define a canonical data model early — even if the software stack stays simple at launch. (See Investor Plan Section 14.4: Canonical Data Model.)


Agent 8: Multi-Market Scale Risk
“Spokane can work perfectly and the scale thesis can still fail. Local success is necessary but not sufficient. The question is whether this model transfers, not just whether it performs.”
Attack 1: The founder model never becomes transferable
Simon’s diagnostic judgment, customer rapport, and quality instincts are embedded in his experience, not in the SOP binder. If the first non-founder technician produces at 50–60% of Simon’s quality and customer satisfaction, the brand deteriorates with every new hire.
VERDICT: A business that only works when the founder is the technician is a job, not a platform.
FIX: Test transferability explicitly: hire the first non-founder tech by month 8–10. Measure their DVI completion, comeback rate, review scores, and ARO against Simon’s. If the gap is >20%, the SOP package needs more work before expansion. (See Investor Plan Section 2B, Staged Roadmap.)
Attack 2: Second market economics fail even though Spokane works
Spokane has specific advantages: low competition (one branded competitor), low cost of living, founder’s personal network for launch pipeline, and first-mover advantage. Boise or Portland may have more competitors, higher marketing costs, no personal network, and different customer expectations. The unit economics that work in Spokane may not replicate.
VERDICT: Every market has different density, competition, weather, and customer behavior. A model validated in one market is a hypothesis in the next.
FIX: Before entering a second market, define the minimum viable pod economics: how many jobs/day per tech at what ARO to break even by month 4? If the second market cannot hit these numbers within 6 months, pull back and diagnose rather than subsidize.
Attack 3: Region-wide winter seasonality breaks utilization
Spokane, Boise, and Tri-Cities all experience significant winter drag (10–25% revenue drop Dec–Feb). If the portfolio is too heavily weighted toward inland markets, winter creates a simultaneous cash crunch across all locations.
VERDICT: A PNW platform that is 100% inland has a structural seasonality problem that no amount of operating discipline can fully solve.
FIX: Sequence expansion to include at least one coastal/mild-winter market (Portland or Seattle) by Stage 3. The coastal markets carry revenue during the months when inland markets dip.
Attack 4: Fleet working-capital burden scales badly
Fleet accounts are attractive at small scale. At $2M+ in fleet revenue across multiple markets, the company is financing $165K–$400K in outstanding receivables at any given time. One slow-paying fleet customer can cascade into parts-supplier payment delays, which can cascade into credit problems.
VERDICT: Fleet is a growth accelerant that quietly becomes a financing problem if collections discipline does not scale with volume.
FIX: Set fleet credit policies before scaling fleet aggressively: credit checks, deposit requirements on first-time accounts, 30-day hard limit with automatic suspension, and a line of credit or factoring arrangement once fleet AR exceeds $100K.
Attack 5: Route density never gets good enough outside the first market
Spokane Valley and South Hill may prove dense enough for 4–5 jobs/day with <20 min drive times. A new market starts at zero density and may take 6–9 months to reach 3 jobs/day per tech. During that ramp, every route is unprofitable. In smaller or more sprawling metros, the density may never reach Spokane levels.
VERDICT: Route density is the single most important economic driver in mobile service. If it does not develop in a new market, every job costs more in windshield time than the model assumes.
FIX: Define a density gate for every new market: if a tech cannot average 3+ jobs/day with <25 min drive times within 6 months, the zone needs to be tightened or the market needs to be reconsidered. Track drive time as rigorously as ARO.


Agent 9: Control Tower and Hiring Risk
“The $10M plan requires 36–40 technicians across 5–6 markets. That means hiring 50–70 people over 3–4 years, and building a centralized operations hub that manages them all without proportional admin growth. Both of these are fundamentally harder than running one van in Spokane.”
Attack 1: Control tower complexity outruns operational discipline
A centralized control tower handling intake, estimates, scheduling, follow-up, and fleet admin across 5+ markets is a serious operational challenge. If scheduling errors, estimate mistakes, or follow-up failures start compounding across markets, the control tower becomes a liability instead of leverage.
VERDICT: Centralized operations save money only if they work reliably. A control tower that creates errors faster than it saves admin hours makes every market worse.
FIX: Scale the control tower incrementally: first prove it works for Spokane alone, then add Boise, then Portland. Each addition should not require more than 0.5–1.0 additional coordinator FTE. If adding a market requires 2+ FTEs, the automation is not working and must be fixed before expanding further.
Attack 2: Hiring average technicians destroys customer trust
The mobile mechanic labor pool is smaller than the shop pool because it requires self-direction, customer communication, and comfort working alone. As the company scales past 10–15 techs, the hiring bar naturally drops. Average mechanics produce average work, which produces average reviews, which destroys the premium positioning.
VERDICT: The #1 bottleneck in scaling mobile service is not demand. It is finding technicians who can represent the brand unsupervised on a customer’s property.
FIX: Never lower the hiring bar. Build a structured pipeline: job posting, phone screen, in-person interview, paid ride-along day, 60-day probation with photo audits and customer follow-up. Accept a 1-in-4 hire rate. Pay above market ($24–$30/hr) to attract better candidates. Track comeback rate, DVI completion, and review scores per technician individually.
Attack 3: Automation never becomes reliable enough to reduce admin headcount
The plan assumes a coordinator-to-tech ratio of 1:6 to 1:8 through automation. If the AI voice intake misclassifies leads, the auto-reminders fire at wrong times, the estimate drafts are inaccurate, or the routing suggestions are poor, coordinators end up fixing automation failures instead of being freed by them. The ratio reverts to 1:3 to 1:4 and the headcount model breaks.
VERDICT: Automation that requires constant human correction is not automation. It is a more complex version of manual work.
FIX: Measure automation reliability as a KPI: what % of auto-generated review requests, deferred-work reminders, and estimate drafts require no human correction? Target: 90%+ reliability before counting on automation to reduce headcount. If reliability is below 80%, the automation is creating work, not eliminating it.
Attack 4: The founders become the bottleneck to their own growth
Simon is the quality standard. Dez is the systems architect. As the company grows, both are pulled into management, hiring, training, and troubleshooting across markets. If Simon spends 50% of his time managing techs instead of wrenching, and Dez spends 50% of his time fighting fires instead of building systems, neither founder is doing what made the company work in the first place.
VERDICT: Founder dependence is the most common cause of stalled growth in field service businesses. The company grows to the founders’ personal capacity and then plateaus.
FIX: By Stage 3 (third market), hire a market lead who owns local operations, QC, and training. Simon’s role shifts from lead technician to technical standards and training. Dez’s role shifts from daily admin to platform development and analytics. Neither founder should be doing daily operational work past $3M–$4M in revenue.


Synthesis: The 15 Most Likely Ways This Company Dies
Aggregated across all nine agents. The first 12 attack the local business. The last 3 attack the scale thesis. If you cannot name the countermeasure, the business is not yet ready.

Early Warning Signs You Are Already Failing
Most businesses do not fail suddenly. They fail gradually and then all at once. The purpose of this list is to catch the gradual part early.
The calendar is full, but take-home pay feels disappointing.
Average repair order rises, but reviews begin using words like “expensive,” “pushy,” or “always finding something.”
Deferred work is being logged, but almost none of it is closing later.
Travel time between jobs keeps rising because the business keeps saying yes outside the zone.
The owner is spending more time on admin and recovery than on controlled production.
Technician or coordinator work creates more questions than it removes.
There are too many versions of the same customer story across texts, invoices, notes, and memory.
The van carries random parts for random service lanes instead of a deliberate stock mix.
Same-day parts runs become normal instead of exceptional.
The business is growing in revenue without growing in gross profit per route hour.

Early Warning Signs: Good Local Business, Bad Scale Business
These signs indicate Spokane is working but the model may not transfer. Watch for these starting in month 6.
Every new customer relationship depends on Simon’s personal rapport, not the system’s reliability.
The SOP binder exists but nobody except Simon actually follows it consistently.
DVI completion and photo quality drop immediately when Simon is not the technician.
Review scores are 4.9 for Simon’s jobs and 4.2 for the second tech’s jobs.
Dez is the only person who understands how the automation works, and it breaks when Dez is unavailable.
Estimates still require Simon’s personal review on 80%+ of jobs after month 9.
The coordinator role cannot be described in a document — it can only be performed by Dez.
Route density only works in the zones Simon personally cultivated. New zones never reach 3+ jobs/day.
Fleet accounts were all closed by Simon’s personal relationships. The outreach process is not replicable.
Revenue grows but the founders’ hours grow faster. The business is scaling effort, not systems.


How to Use This Pre-Mortem Weekly
Read the synthesis table once a week and identify which failure mode feels closest right now.
Review the early-warning list every Friday against the real numbers from the dashboard.
If two or more warning signs persist for four consecutive weeks, stop chasing growth and fix the underlying system.
Treat this document as living operating doctrine. When a new failure mode appears in the real world, add it here.
