## The business in one sentence

WrenchReady is not mainly a mobile mechanic website.

It is a promise-keeping vehicle service business that must reduce uncertainty better than the alternatives and turn kept promises into repeat, deferred, and recurring revenue.

## The main company goal

Create demand, make clear promises, keep them visibly, create happy customers, and turn that trust into repeat, referral, and recurring revenue.

## The main build goal

Give WrenchReady one operating spine for:

- inbound
- promise
- readiness
- field execution
- closeout
- recapture
- collections
- warranty handling
- recurring-account growth

## What the benchmark companies taught us

The benchmark companies were not important because they were "apps."

They mattered because they made the customer feel that the promise was real.

The shared pattern was:

1. make the promise specific
2. make the promise believable
3. show progress toward the promise
4. make recovery clear when the promise is at risk
5. make repeat use easier than switching away

This is true for Domino's, Uber, Airbnb, DoorDash, Instacart, and the rest.

That is the standard WrenchReady should chase.

## What the pricing research taught us

WrenchReady should not try to win by being vague, cheap, or broad.

It should win with:

- understandable, profitable service wedges
- visible pricing rules
- paid diagnosis where uncertainty is real
- minimum-ticket and travel discipline
- strong conversion into the next profitable step

The strongest early economic lanes are still:

- no-start / battery / charging-system work
- brakes
- starter / alternator
- paid inspections
- recurring account and fleet-style preventive maintenance

The weak lane as a primary wedge is still:

- standalone oil changes

Oil changes can still matter, but more as:

- route-fill
- retention
- bundle logic
- fleet maintenance

## The commercial doctrine

WrenchReady must keep three different things separate:

1. what we market
2. what we dispatch
3. what we aim to convert into

That means:

- a service can be a strong marketing offer without being a blind dispatch-first job
- a visit can be economically justified by what it leads to, not just by the first line item
- the CRM must store both the attraction logic and the conversion logic

## The operating doctrine

The promise is the product.

A promise means things like:

- we will respond quickly
- we will tell you clearly whether we can help
- we will give you a real arrival window
- we will update you if the plan changes
- we will explain scope changes before cost changes
- we will close the loop after the visit

If those promises are not specific, they cannot be kept.

If they are not kept, trust does not compound.

If trust does not compound, the business stays stuck in one-off local chaos.

## The current standard

Every meaningful build should strengthen these standards:

- the promise must be specific enough to operationalize
- the customer must be able to feel certainty and progress
- risk must be visible before the promise breaks
- execution truth must outrank stale board labels or manual bucket drift
- every finished visit should create proof, recap, and the next probable visit
- no channel should be treated as live if compliance or transport reality says otherwise
- no growth surface should be allowed to create equal growth in office chaos

## The current software center

The software center is still the Promise CRM and its surrounding operating views:

- inbound queue
- promise record
- Promise Board
- tomorrow readiness
- follow-through
- closeout and recapture
- outbound queue
- recapture scorecard
- proof discipline
- recurring accounts
- operating cadence
- systems readiness
- wedge focus
- management review
- proposal and trial discipline inside the recurring-account lane
- closeout quality measurement, not just closeout completion
- queue-first field, warranty, and recapture actions so operators can improve the system where the work is surfaced
- public proof stories sourced from permission-safe closeouts instead of fabricated testimonials

This is the right center because it is the shortest path from:

- demand
- to promise
- to kept promise
- to repeat trust
- to recurring revenue

## The next six big steps

These are the next big steps now that the core operating spine, production-safe email, and test-mode deposit and remaining-balance flows are live:

1. finish the review destination and public trust loop
2. switch payments from test-mode proof to live-mode operating reality
3. make the weekly recapture and operator cadence a real management habit
4. turn the recurring-account lane into real outreach, trials, and repeat accounts
5. use the now-live wedge focus weekly so no-start and brake demand stay sharper than generic mobile-mechanic demand
6. activate text only when 10DLC and transport reality make it honest to do so

Why these six:

- the benchmark work says customers return when the promise is visible, the recovery is clear, and repeat use is easy
- the pricing work says the business must protect margin and convert the first visit into the right next step
- the current build now makes trust, payments, recap, review, and follow-through real enough that the next gains come from execution depth, not more software sprawl
- the recurring-account work still matters because it changes the business story faster than only adding one-off consumer demand

## What should not distract the team now

Do not confuse motion with progress.

Things that should stay secondary until the operating machine is tighter:

- full native consumer app work
- broad marketplace logic
- broad service-catalog expansion
- AI output that does not reduce labor or risk
- payment-surface complexity before one clear processor is chosen
- BNPL before the base payment flow is real

## What success should look like

In the near term:

- cleaner inbound
- more believable promises
- fewer tomorrow surprises
- stronger closeout discipline
- more review and recap follow-through
- better proof capture
- a visible collection lane
- a visible warranty lane
- one real recurring-account lane

In the medium term:

- profitable service days
- lower office touches per job
- stronger repeat and deferred-work revenue
- stronger customer trust signals
- a business that looks increasingly like an operating system, not a hustle
