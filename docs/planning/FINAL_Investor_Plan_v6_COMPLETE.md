OPERATING PHILOSOPHY

Two principles govern every decision in this company.

EXTERNALLY
“Earn the next visit.”
Every customer interaction — every inspection, every recommendation, every price,
every follow-up, every comeback — is measured by one question:
did this earn us the right to see this customer again?
INTERNALLY
“Why isn’t Simon on a wrench?”
Every system, every hire, every process, every tool, every minute of admin work
exists to answer one question:
is Simon producing billable wrench-hours right now, and if not, why not?

The company has two people and two jobs. Simon’s job is to be on a wrench — performing excellent work, doing thorough inspections, presenting findings honestly, and earning the next visit from every customer he touches. The admin partner’s job is to make sure nothing prevents Simon from being on a wrench — handling intake, scheduling, routing, parts ordering, follow-up, marketing, fleet outreach, review management, deferred-work reminders, bookkeeping, and system building. Every dollar of revenue traces back to Simon’s wrench time. Every system exists to protect it.

THE NORTH STAR
“Spokane is the lab. The Pacific Northwest is the market.”
Every process, every system, every automation is built so that any qualified technician
can be inserted into a strong operating system and produce at 80%+ of founder quality.
The company we are building is a regional operating system, not a local repair business.

If a decision earns the next visit and protects Simon’s wrench time, do it.
If a decision risks customer trust or pulls Simon off a wrench, stop and reconsider.
Everything else in this plan follows from these two principles.

2. Business Thesis and Competitive Wedge
The business sells friction reduction for common vehicle maintenance and light repair. The customer avoids the waiting room, tow bill, ride coordination, and time off work associated with a traditional shop visit. The company wins on speed, convenience, communication, and route efficiency — not on breadth of service.
Competitive landscape — Spokane County
Direct mobile competitor: Sypher’s Mobile Mechanic — ASE-certified, 15+ years experience, published rates of $120/hr domestic, $160/hr European, $200/hr diesel, $150 diagnostic fee. Serves Spokane, Spokane Valley, and Liberty Lake. This is the only established, branded mobile mechanic operation in the market.

6.8 Pricing Strategy Insights — How to Maximize GP/hr
These 12 pricing rules should govern every estimate, every upsell conversation, and every scheduling decision. They are derived from the parts cost data above and from best practices across successful mobile repair operations.
Rule 1 — Value-based pricing, not cost-plus. You are not selling parts + labor. You are selling convenience, no tow, no waiting room, no time off work. Price accordingly.
Rule 2 — Three-tier oil changes. Conventional ($65) / Synthetic Blend ($80) / Full Synthetic ($110). The middle tier anchors most customers to $80 instead of $65. Add the premium 25-pt inspection tier at $110–$140 as the “best value.”
Rule 3 — Package brake jobs. Quote “Complete Brake Service — $380” (CR-V class) or “$550” (F-250 class), not line-item parts + labor. Packages eliminate price-shopping on individual parts.
Rule 4 — Fluid services are hidden gold. Brake fluid flush: $8–$15 in fluid, 30–45 min, $100–$140 customer price = 88–92% margin. Test brake fluid moisture on every oil change. It’s the easiest $100+ upsell in the business.
Rule 5 — Bundle add-on pricing. Standalone cabin filter: $55. Add-on during oil change: $35. Your incremental time is 5–7 minutes and you’re already on-site. Same GP, lower customer price, higher close rate.
Rule 6 — Free battery testing is a sales engine. Test every battery on every visit. Print the result. “Weak” or “Replace” = instant $180–$280 sale with zero selling required.
Rule 7 — Tiered diagnostics. Code scan + quick assessment: $95. Full diagnostic: $150. Extended/complex: $200. All credit toward repairs. Captures quick jobs that refuse $150 and properly charges for 90-min electrical gremlins.
Rule 8 — Vehicle-class pricing. CR-V brake job: $350–$420. F-250 brake job: $550–$700. Your parts cost difference is 2–3×. Your pricing must reflect this. Never quote a flat price across vehicle classes.
Rule 9 — Variable markup by price visibility. Batteries, starters, alternators (customer can google price): 25–30% markup. Hardware kits, gaskets, clips, fluid (customer can’t easily price): 60–100% markup.
Rule 10 — Trip fee waiver as a sales tool. “If we add the brake fluid flush today, your total hits $370 and the trip fee is waived.” You add $100+ in high-margin work, customer feels they got a deal.
Rule 11 — Maintenance packages. Annual plan: 2 synthetic oil changes + 1 tire rotation + 1 brake inspection + filter check = $299/year (normally $350+). Locks in repeat revenue and 2 guaranteed upsell visits.
Rule 12 — “While I’m here” pricing is the #1 GP/hr amplifier. Every minute of additional work done while already on-site is nearly 100% incremental profit. An $85 oil change that becomes $195 with add-ons increased your effective rate from $94/hr to $143/hr for 12 extra minutes of work.

8. Mobile Reality Adjustments
The following adjustments correct for assumptions that were based on shop conditions, ideal weather, or best-case scenarios. Each is quantified with its impact on revenue stated. An investor should see these as evidence the plan has been stress-tested.
8.1 Labor Time Multiplier (1.3×–1.5× on undercar work)
Standard flat-rate guides assume a lift, pneumatic tools, overhead lighting, and flat concrete. Mobile driveway work is slower: jack-up/stand cycles add 5–10 min per job, working on a creeper is 30–50% slower than at a lift, cordless tools are slightly slower on seized fasteners, and every job ends with driveway cleanup. On Spokane vehicles 10+ years old with winter road-salt corrosion, the multiplier can reach 1.5–2.0×.
Impact: Effective GP/hr is reduced ~26% vs. shop calculations. Pricing in this plan already compensates — the posted labor rate delivers target margins at mobile speeds.
8.2 Spokane Weather (15–22 reduced-productivity days/year)
44 inches of snowfall/year, primarily December–February.
34 days/year where the high never exceeds 32°F. 132 frosty nights, September–May.
Winter highs: Dec 33°F, Jan 34°F, Feb 39°F. Summer: excellent (80–88°F, <0.5" rain/month).
Estimated impact: 8–12 full days lost + 8–10 reduced-productivity days = 15–22 affected days/year.
Mitigation: schedule fleet work in covered locations on worst days; use pop-up canopy for rain; shift winter scheduling to midday; run seasonal promotions; revenue model uses 50 weeks not 52.
Sources: NWS Spokane Airport (GEG) climatological reports. Weather Spark Spokane climate data. Visit Spokane weather summaries. Current Results — Spokane monthly temperature averages.
8.3 Parts-Run Time Drain (~$10,400/yr potential loss)
In a shop, parts are delivered while you wrench. Mobile, YOU are the runner. Wrong/missing/additional parts mid-job can cost 30–60 minutes of dead time per occurrence. At 2 parts runs per 5-day week × 45 min each = 90 min/week lost = ~$10,400/year at $120/hr.
Mitigation: Pre-order and confirm fitment before arriving. Stock top 20 common parts in the van. Open commercial accounts at two closest O’Reilly and AutoZone for same-day will-call. Add 15-min buffer between jobs.
8.4 Site Quality Variability
Not every driveway is level, paved, or safe to jack on. Screen during intake: “Is your driveway paved and relatively flat?”
Carry jack pads for soft surfaces. Decline jobs on unsafe sites — liability issue.
Apartment/HOA restrictions: suggest customer move vehicle to safe nearby location.
8.5 Billable Hour Efficiency
Shop tech efficiency: 6–8 billable hours in an 8-hour day (75–100%). Mobile reality: drive time (15–30 min/transit), setup/teardown (10–15 min/job), customer communication (5–10 min/job), daily admin (30–60 min). Realistic mobile efficiency: 5–6.5 billable hours in an 8–10 hour day. This plan uses 4.5–6 hrs — conservative and honest.
8.6 Seasonal Revenue Fluctuation
Peak (May–Oct): 110–120% of monthly average. Shoulder (Mar–Apr, Nov): 90–100%. Slow (Dec–Feb): 70–85%.
A spring launch aligns ramp-up with improving weather. A fall/winter launch makes $400K significantly harder.
Build cash reserves during peak months. Use winter for fleet prospecting, tool upgrades, content creation.
Sources: Spokane seasonal climate data (NWS, Weather Spark). Mobile mechanic seasonal patterns from YourMechanic, Wrench, and AutoNation Mobile Service operational consensus.


9. Revenue Maximization Engine
This section explains how the business increases revenue per visit while protecting customer trust, review quality, and long-term brand strength. The model is NOT a high-pressure upsell system. It is an inspection-led, evidence-based approach that turns every maintenance visit into a trust event, a diagnostic opportunity, and a legitimate revenue growth moment.
The goal is NOT “turn every oil change into a $500 ticket.” The goal IS: frequent oil visits becoming $150–$250 tickets through legitimate add-ons, some becoming $250–$400 when the vehicle genuinely needs additional work, and a smaller percentage becoming $400–$600+ when multiple real needs converge — backed by evidence, not pressure.
9.1 Oil Change Monetization Model
Oil changes are not commodity work. They are the single most important service in the business model because they serve five simultaneous functions: trust builder, inspection opportunity, review generator, deferred-work generator, and customer acquisition event. The oil change is the gateway to the entire revenue engine.
Oil visit workflow
Step 1: Customer books oil service. This is your lowest-friction entry point. Easy to say yes to.
Step 2: Perform oil service plus structured 18-point inspection (see Section 9.2). Every oil change includes this. No exceptions.
Step 3: Identify only evidence-backed findings. Battery test printout, brake pad measurement, visual filter condition, tread depth reading, fluid condition. No guessing.
Step 4: Present findings in Now / Soon / Monitor format (see Section 9.4). Photograph evidence where possible.
Step 5: Customer chooses: do it now (same-visit add-on), schedule it (deferred work — booked appointment), or monitor it (logged for future follow-up).
Step 6: Completed or deferred work enters the follow-up pipeline in your shop management software. Reminders are sent at appropriate intervals.
Oil visit Average Repair Order (ARO) ladder
This is a realistic operating framework, not a guarantee. The distribution will vary by customer mix, vehicle age, and season.

At a realistic blended ARO of $140–$175 per oil visit (not the $85–$110 base), an owner doing 3 oil changes per day generates $420–$525/day from oil work alone — before any standalone repair or diagnostic appointments. This is why oil changes are the foundation, not an afterthought.
9.2 Mandatory Inspection Checklist — Every Maintenance Visit
This checklist is performed on every oil change, every maintenance visit, and every diagnostic appointment. It takes 8–12 minutes and is the engine of both trust and revenue. Findings are documented with photos and presented to the customer before leaving the site.
☐ Battery test (digital tester — CCA, voltage, health status). Print or screenshot result.
☐ Brake visual check (pad thickness estimate through wheel spokes or with wheel removed during rotation).
☐ Tire tread depth (measured with gauge, all 4 tires) + tire condition (cracking, uneven wear, bulges).
☐ Wiper blade condition (visual check for cracking, streaking, missing sections).
☐ Cabin air filter condition (remove and inspect — show dirty filter to customer if replacement needed).
☐ Engine air filter condition (remove and inspect — hold up to light test).
☐ Fluid levels and condition: coolant (color, level), brake fluid (color, moisture test strip if available), power steering (level, color), transmission (if accessible dipstick).
☐ Visible leaks: oil, coolant, power steering, brake fluid, transmission. Note location and severity.
☐ Belts and hoses: serpentine belt (cracking, glazing, fraying), visible coolant hoses (soft spots, swelling, leaks).
☐ Exterior lights: headlights, taillights, brake lights, turn signals, license plate light. Test all.
☐ Obvious suspension/steering: visible torn boots, leaking shocks, loose tie rods (grab-and-shake if wheel is off).
☐ Dashboard warning lights: check engine light, ABS, TPMS, airbag, battery, oil pressure. Note any active lights.
☐ Service history context: if customer shares history, note overdue intervals. If no history available, note as unknown.
☐ Windshield condition: chips, cracks (note for customer — insurance may cover repair before it spreads).

This inspection is not a sales pitch. It is a professional service that builds trust, creates documentation, and identifies real needs. The customer receives a written or digital summary of findings with photos. This summary IS the marketing — it’s what earns 5-star reviews, referrals, and repeat visits.
9.3 Evidence-Based Recommendations — The Trust Guardrail
This company does NOT recommend add-on work unless there is verifiable evidence. This is a non-negotiable operating principle. Recommending work without evidence destroys trust, generates 1-star reviews, and kills repeat business.
Acceptable evidence for recommendations
Battery test printout or screen showing “WEAK” or “REPLACE” status with CCA reading.
Brake pad measurement (mm remaining) or photo showing pad thickness vs. backing plate.
Tread depth gauge reading at 4/32" or below (approaching replacement) or 2/32" (safety concern).
Dirty cabin or engine air filter held next to a clean replacement for visual comparison.
Brake fluid moisture test strip showing elevated water content (>3% = recommend flush).
Visible cracked, glazed, or fraying serpentine belt or swollen/soft coolant hose.
Owner’s manual maintenance interval showing overdue service (e.g., coolant flush every 30K/2 years).
Service history gap: customer confirms no record of a flush or service and interval has been exceeded.
Visible active leak with identifiable fluid type and location.
Stored or active diagnostic trouble codes with supporting test results.
What we will NOT do
No “blanket fluid flush” selling. Fluid services are recommended only when supported by interval, test result, contamination evidence, or unknown service history.
No quoting large repair conclusions from symptoms alone without a proper diagnostic inspection.
No recommending services just because they are profitable. Every recommendation must serve the customer’s vehicle.
9.4 Now / Soon / Monitor Presentation Framework
Every inspection finding is classified into one of three urgency buckets. This framework prevents the business from overwhelming customers with a long list of recommendations and preserves trust and review quality.
NOW — Address today
Safety issues: brake pads at or below 2mm, bald tires, active brake fluid leak, failed battery.
Likely-to-fail items: battery testing “REPLACE,” serpentine belt with visible cracking and age, severely contaminated fluid.
High-confidence same-visit opportunities: dirty cabin/engine filter with replacement on the van, weak battery with replacement in stock, worn wipers in rain season.
SOON — Schedule within 30–90 days
Brake pads at 3–4mm — not urgent today but will need replacement within a few months.
Coolant that is dark or overdue by interval but not yet causing problems.
Tires at 4/32" — functional but should be replaced before winter or next long trip.
Serpentine belt showing early wear signs but not yet at risk of failure.
MONITOR — Document and revisit
Minor seepage (not a drip or active leak) at a gasket — note it, photograph it, check again next visit.
Brake pads at 5–6mm — plenty of life left, but the customer should know they’re not new.
Slight tire wear pattern that may indicate alignment — worth mentioning but not actionable yet.
Battery testing “GOOD” but 4+ years old — note the age for future awareness.

Present only the top 1–3 most relevant actions during a visit. Do NOT bury the customer in 10 findings at once. If there are 8 things to mention, prioritize the NOW items, briefly mention the SOON items, and save the MONITOR items for the written report. The customer should leave feeling informed, not overwhelmed.
9.5 Increasing Average Repair Order Without Hurting Trust
These are the specific, ethical strategies for increasing revenue per visit. Each one is designed to add value for the customer while improving GP/hr for the business.
A. Bundle logically
Oil + engine air filter: natural pair. “Your filter is pretty dirty — I can swap it right now for $35 since I’m already here.”
Oil + cabin filter: same logic. Show the dirty filter. Let the customer decide.
Oil + wiper blades: especially in fall/winter Spokane. “Your wipers are streaking — I have a set that fits, $40 installed.”
Oil + tire rotation: “Since I have the jack out anyway, I can rotate your tires for $30.”
Brake service + brake fluid flush: “Since we’re already in the brake system, this is the best time to do the fluid.”
Battery replacement + terminal cleaning + charging system verification: natural package.
B. Same-visit convenience pricing
When you are already on-site, your incremental cost for an add-on is near zero — no additional travel, no additional setup. This allows you to ethically offer a lower price on add-ons during the same visit, which increases the customer’s willingness to say yes while maintaining strong margins.
“Cabin filter is $55 by itself as a separate appointment. Since I’m already here, I can do it for $35.”
“If we do the brake fluid service today, I’ll waive the trip fee — saves you $25.”
This is not discounting. This is capturing revenue that would otherwise require a separate trip.
C. Vehicle-class pricing
Trucks, full-size SUVs, and HD vehicles must be priced 30–60% higher than compact vehicles for the same service. Parts cost 2–3× more (F-250 brake pads $90–$130 vs. CR-V $35–$45) and labor takes longer due to heavier components. Never quote flat prices across vehicle classes.
D. Maintenance packages
30K Service Package: synthetic oil + all filters (engine, cabin) + tire rotation + brake inspection + fluid condition check. Price: $199–$249.
60K Service Package: everything in 30K + serpentine belt inspection + spark plug check + coolant flush if overdue. Price: $349–$449.
Used Vehicle Baseline Package: full synthetic oil + all filters + brake fluid flush + battery test + comprehensive inspection + tire check. Price: $249–$299. Ideal for recent used-car purchases.
Annual Maintenance Plan: 2 synthetic oil changes + 1 tire rotation + 1 brake inspection + filter checks + priority scheduling. Price: $279–$329/year.
E. Deferred-work capture
Not every dollar must close today. A high-trust system captures future revenue by documenting declined or deferred work and following up at appropriate intervals.
Log every SOON and MONITOR finding in your shop management software with the customer’s vehicle record.
Send a reminder at 30 days for SOON items and at 6 months for MONITOR items.
“Hi [Name], when we serviced your CR-V last month we noted your brake pads were getting thin. Want to get those taken care of before they start grinding? I have openings this week.”
Deferred-work conversion rate target: 25–40% of documented SOON items should convert within 90 days.
F. Minimum-ticket discipline
Do not accept standalone jobs that fall below your service-call minimum ($150) unless they are add-ons to another service.
A standalone cabin filter replacement ($55) is a money-losing trip. But a cabin filter added to an oil change ($35 incremental) is one of your highest GP/hr services.
If a customer calls for a single low-ticket item, offer to bundle it with their next maintenance service or apply the service-call minimum.
9.6 Best Add-Ons Ranked by GP/hr
These are the highest-value same-visit add-ons, ranked by incremental GP/hr. They share common traits: short time, visual evidence, easy customer comprehension, strong margin, and low comeback risk.

Important: not every profitable add-on is a good add-on. A serpentine belt adds $180+ to the ticket but takes 20–40 minutes and introduces parts/complexity risk. An air filter adds $35–$50 in 3–5 minutes with zero risk. The air filter is the better GP/hr play almost every time. Optimize for short, safe, visual add-ons first.
9.7 Revenue Guardrails — What We Will Not Do
These guardrails exist to protect customer trust, review quality, and the long-term brand. They are as important as any pricing strategy.
We do not pressure customers into unnecessary work.
We do not present every finding as urgent. The Now / Soon / Monitor framework exists for a reason.
We do not recommend flushes without evidence or interval support.
We do not quote large repair conclusions from symptoms alone without proper diagnosis.
We do not bury customers in 10 recommendations at once. Top 1–3 actions per visit.
We do not use manipulative urgency language (“this could fail any day”) unless the evidence genuinely supports imminent failure.
We protect long-term trust over same-day revenue. A customer who trusts you will return 4–6 times per year for years. A customer who feels pushed will leave a 3-star review and never call again.
If in doubt about whether a recommendation is appropriate, err on the side of MONITOR rather than NOW.
9.8 Customer Communication Scripts for Ethical Recommendations
These scripts are designed for real conversations with customers. They sound professional, helpful, and non-pushy. Use them as templates and adapt to your natural speaking style.
Presenting inspection findings
“Hey [Name], your oil’s all done and everything looks good overall. I did find a couple of things I want to show you. Your cabin air filter is pretty clogged — here, take a look [show filter]. And your battery tested at 340 CCA out of a rated 500 — it’s getting weak. Neither is an emergency today, but the battery could leave you stranded on a cold Spokane morning. Want me to take care of either of those while I’m here?”
Offering a same-visit add-on
“Since I’m already here and have everything set up, I can swap that filter for $35 instead of the usual $55 for a separate visit. Takes about five minutes.”
Explaining Now / Soon / Monitor
“So here’s what I found: your front brake pads are getting low — I’d say about 3mm left. They’re safe for now, but you’ll want to get those done in the next month or two before they start making noise. Your engine air filter is fine. And your battery is strong. I’ll put the brake pads on your file and send you a reminder in about 30 days. Sound good?”
Handling a declined recommendation
“Totally understand. I’ll note it in your file so we can keep an eye on it next time. No pressure at all — it’s your call.”
Battery test to battery sale
“Your battery tested at 340 cold cranking amps out of a rated 500. That’s in the ‘weak’ zone — it’ll probably get you through summer, but with Spokane winters, I wouldn’t want to gamble on it starting in December. Brake fluid recommendation

The essential closing line
“You don’t have to do all of this today. I just want to make sure you know what I found so you can make the best decision for your vehicle and your budget.”

10.4 Why Customers Will Actually Choose This Business
Customer psychology matters as much as pricing. People choose a mobile mechanic over a traditional shop for specific reasons, and the business must deliver on every one of them.
Convenience: the vehicle is serviced where it already sits — home, work, apartment. No drive, no drop-off, no ride coordination, no waiting room.
No tow required: for no-start, dead battery, or drivability-impaired vehicles, mobile eliminates the $100–$200 tow bill entirely.
Less time off work: the customer can have their car serviced in their driveway or parking lot while they work. A shop visit costs 2–4 hours of their day.
Transparency: digital inspection photos, condition reports, and written estimates sent via text or email. The customer sees what you see.
Trust through evidence: the Now / Soon / Monitor framework (Section 9.4) builds trust by showing the customer you are NOT trying to sell them everything at once.
Honest recommendation culture: the customer learns quickly that you only recommend work backed by evidence (Section 9.3). This earns 5-star reviews and referrals.
Same-visit convenience for add-ons: the customer can say yes to a filter swap or battery replacement on the spot, without scheduling another appointment.
Speed: most maintenance and light repair services are completed in 30–90 minutes. The customer gets their vehicle back faster than any shop.

14. Service and Repair Documentation Architecture
All service and repair documentation must live in one operational system of record from day one. This section defines what must be stored, where it should live, and how to think about future platform scale without overbuilding early.
14.1 What Must Be Stored
Every interaction with a customer or vehicle must be documented in a searchable, permanent system. This is not optional — it is required for RCW 46.71 compliance, warranty defense, comeback resolution, deferred-work follow-up, and repeat customer management.
Customer record: name, phone, email, address, communication preferences, referral source.
Vehicle record: year, make, model, engine, VIN, license plate, odometer at each visit, known history.
Appointment history: dates, services requested, services performed, time on-site, technician assigned.
Estimates: itemized parts and labor, customer authorization status (approved/declined/deferred), date and method of authorization.
Repair orders: work performed, parts used (with part numbers and source), labor time, torque specs if applicable, technician notes.
Digital inspections: structured checklist results, photos (pre-work, mid-work, post-work), condition findings.
Invoices and payment status: itemized invoice, payment method, date collected, outstanding balance if any.
Warranty notes: parts warranty terms, labor warranty terms, comeback history if any.
Deferred work: documented SOON and MONITOR findings with target follow-up dates.
Next recommended service: mileage-based or time-based maintenance intervals, scheduled reminders.
Review and follow-up status: review request sent (Y/N), review received (Y/N), follow-up reminder scheduled.
Fleet notes: PO numbers, billing contact, fleet vehicle list, service schedule, account terms.
14.2 System of Record — Day 1 Architecture
The recommended day-one architecture is simple and opinionated: use an existing shop-management or field-service platform as the system of record. Do NOT try to build a full custom CRM or shop operating system from scratch before proving demand.
Why use an existing platform
Repair orders, estimates, digital inspections, approvals, invoices, customer history, audit trails, reminders, and payment workflows already exist in proven systems like AutoLeap, Tekmetric, Shop-Ware, or similar.
These systems are designed for RCW 46.71 compliance (itemized estimates, authorization tracking, invoice requirements).
Building a custom system from scratch before you have 100 customers is a distraction that consumes capital, time, and attention that should go to serving customers and building revenue.
The correct day-one decision is: pick a reliable system, learn it deeply, and use it for everything.
What to build custom (thin intelligence layer)
Build a thin intelligence and automation layer AROUND the system of record, not a replacement for it. This custom layer can handle:
Intake triage: pre-qualifying leads before they enter the booking system.
Estimate drafting: AI-assisted estimate generation from diagnostic notes and voice memos.
Deferred-work follow-up: automated reminders for SOON items at 30/60/90 days.
Now / Soon / Monitor summaries: formatted customer-facing reports from inspection data.
ARO analytics: tracking average repair order by visit type, customer segment, and technician.
Route optimization: daily route planning layered on top of the appointment calendar.
Fleet/account workflows: recurring service scheduling and fleet billing automation.
Reporting dashboards: pulling data from the system of record into custom KPI views.

26. Operational Protocols
These protocols address the highest-risk operational scenarios identified during the adversarial pre-mortem review. Each protocol should be printed and kept in the van as a laminated reference card.
26.1 First-Hire Controls
Run 2–3 paid ride-along assessment days before making an offer. Pay the candidate for their time. Watch habits, communication, and quality under real conditions.
Restrict the new technician to Tier 1 repairs ONLY for the first 60 days. No diagnostics, no Tier 2/3 work until verified competent.
Require photo documentation of every job: pre-repair, mid-repair, post-repair. Review these daily for the first 30 days, weekly thereafter.
Service coordinator calls every customer within 24 hours of the technician’s job to verify quality and communication.
90-day probationary period in the offer letter with clear performance metrics: jobs/day, comeback rate, review scores, on-time arrival.
Install GPS tracking on the second van for route compliance and arrival/departure verification.
26.2 Site Safety and Property Protection
Take 2–3 photos of the driveway/work area BEFORE placing any equipment. Note pre-existing condition in job notes.
Place a ground mat or drip tray under the vehicle before any fluid-related work.
Use jack pads on soft or finished surfaces to distribute weight.
Establish a visible work zone: place orange cones or barrier around the vehicle.
Politely ask customer to keep children and pets away from the work area.
Never allow a customer to assist with the repair, even if they offer.
Carry a first aid kit in the van.
26.3 Brake Comeback Prevention Protocol
Brake work is the #1 volume repair and the #1 source of liability claims for mobile mechanics. This protocol is mandatory for every brake job.
Torque every lug nut and caliper bracket bolt to manufacturer spec with a calibrated torque wrench. No impact-gun-and-hope.
Bed the brakes before leaving: 5 moderate stops from 35 mph to transfer pad material to rotors.
Photograph the torque wrench at final spec on at least one wheel per axle.
Send the customer a check-in text within 48 hours asking about brake feel.
Offer a free re-inspection at 500 miles. This costs 15 minutes and prevents a $1,000+ comeback and 1-star review.
26.4 Mid-Job Discovery Protocol
When you discover additional problems during a repair (e.g., brake job reveals a seized caliper or cracked flex hose):
STOP work on the additional finding. Do not proceed without authorization.
Present the finding to the customer using your RCW 46.71-compliant additional-findings authorization form.
Document: date, time, finding description, estimated additional cost, customer’s decision (approve/decline/defer).
If the part is not on the van, offer two options: (1) wait 30 minutes while you source it, or (2) reschedule the additional work.
If the customer declines the additional work, document their refusal and note any safety implications.
26.5 Spill Response Protocol
Minor spill (under 1 quart, on driveway): absorb immediately with pads/granules from your spill kit. Sweep and bag. Clean area with degreaser if stained. Photograph before and after.
Major spill (over 1 quart or any amount reaching a storm drain): contain the spread immediately. If it has entered a storm drain, call Spokane County stormwater hotline. Do NOT attempt to wash it down. Document everything. Notify your insurance carrier.
ALWAYS place drip tray under the drain plug BEFORE loosening. This prevents 95% of driveway spills.


27. Operating Playbooks — Scripts, Checklists, and Decision Trees
This appendix contains the operational scripts and checklists referenced throughout the plan. Each playbook should be printed, laminated, and kept in the van or loaded into your shop management software.
Playbook 1: Lead Intake Script
“Thanks for calling [Business Name]. My name is [Owner]. How can I help you today?”
What’s going on with your vehicle? (Let them describe the symptom in their own words.)
What’s the year, make, and model? How many miles?
Where is the vehicle located right now? (Confirm address and surface type — paved driveway, parking lot, etc.)
Has anyone already looked at it or pulled any codes?
Decision: Does this fit our service lanes? Is the site safe? Is the location in our zone?
If YES: “Great, I can help with that. Let me get you scheduled. My diagnostic fee is $150, which covers a full inspection and gets credited toward any repairs we do on-site.”
If NO (wrong service lane): “That’s outside what I handle mobile, but I can recommend [referral shop]. They’re good people.”
If NO (out of zone): “I’m currently servicing [zone area]. If you can get the vehicle to [location], I’d be happy to help. Otherwise I’d recommend [local referral].”
Playbook 2: Pre-Work Site Checklist
☐ Arrived on time. Confirmed with customer.
☐ Photos taken of driveway/work area (2–3 shots showing pre-existing condition).
☐ Ground mat / drip tray placed under vehicle.
☐ Jack pads positioned if needed for surface type.
☐ Cones / work zone markers placed.
☐ Customer informed: keep children/pets clear of work area.

☐ Inspection findings presented to customer (now / soon / monitor format).

☐ Payment collected.
☐ Review request text sent (direct Google review link).

☐ Replaced parts returned to customer if requested.
Playbook 4: Review Request Script
Send via text within 10 minutes of job completion:
“Hi [Name], thanks for choosing [Business Name] today! If you were happy with the service, a Google review would mean the world to us. It takes 30 seconds: [direct review link]. Thank you! — [Owner Name]”
If no response within 48 hours, send a single follow-up:
“Hi [Name], just checking in — how’s the [vehicle] running after yesterday’s work? If you have a moment, a quick Google review would really help us out: [link]. Thanks again!”
Playbook 5: Fleet Outreach Script
For cold walk-in visits to local businesses with vehicle fleets:
“Hi, my name is [Name] and I run [Business Name], a mobile auto repair service here in Spokane. I noticed your company runs [trucks/vans] and I wanted to introduce myself. I service commercial vehicles on-site — at your lot, on your schedule — so your vehicles never have to leave and your crew keeps working. I’d love to offer a free vehicle health check on a couple of your trucks, no obligation, just to show you what we can do. Here’s my card and our fleet services sheet. Who would be the best person to talk to about your vehicle maintenance?”
Playbook 6: Comeback Handling SOP
Customer calls with a post-repair issue: respond within 2 hours. Express concern, not defensiveness.
Schedule a re-inspection within 24 hours at no charge.
If the issue is related to your repair: fix it at no charge. Apologize. Document everything.
If the issue is unrelated to your repair: explain what you found, show the customer, and provide a new estimate for the separate issue.
After every comeback resolution: review the original job documentation. Identify what went wrong. Update the relevant SOP/checklist to prevent recurrence.
Track comeback rate as a weekly metric. Target: below 2%. If above 3% for 2 consecutive weeks, stop and audit.
