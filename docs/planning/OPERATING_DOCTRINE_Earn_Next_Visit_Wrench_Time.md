1. The Two Principles That Govern This Company
This document converts two operating principles into a complete management system. These principles are not slogans. They are decision filters that govern every hire, every process, every tool choice, and every minute of every workday.
Principle 1: Earn the Next Visit
Every completed job must increase the probability of the next booked job from that customer. This is the external governing principle. It applies to every customer interaction, every recommendation, every price, every follow-up, and every comeback.
What it operationally requires
Honest, evidence-based work recommendations. Never optimize for one invoice at the expense of the relationship.
Clear communication: the customer understands what was done, what was found, and what they should consider next.
Proper documentation: inspection photos, written findings, digital estimates, and clean invoices.
Review requests sent within 10 minutes of every completed job.
Follow-up: 48-hour post-service check-in for brake, drivability, and electrical work.
Next-service scheduling: every customer leaves with a maintenance reminder in the system.
Now / Soon / Monitor recommendation framework: never bury the customer in 10 findings. Present the top 1–3, defer the rest, and follow up later.
Deferred-work pipeline: every documented SOON item gets a 30-day reminder. Every MONITOR item gets a 6-month reminder.
The test: if a customer tells their neighbor about the experience, would they describe it as helpful and honest, or as a sales pitch? If the answer is sales pitch, the principle has been violated.
Principle 2: Why Isn’t Simon on a Wrench?
Simon’s wrench time is the single most valuable production asset in the company. Every dollar of revenue traces directly to Simon’s billable hours. Every minute he spends NOT wrenching must be justified, delegated, automated, systemized, or eliminated.
The decision rule
Every non-wrench task must be classified as one of the following. If it cannot be justified under one of these categories, the business has failed to build the right system.

The admin partner’s job description in one sentence: make sure Simon is on a wrench. Every system, hire, and tool investment is measured by whether it adds a billable wrench-hour to Simon’s day or removes a non-wrench minute from it.


2. The Four Buckets — Classifying All Work in the Business
Every task that exists in this business belongs in one of four buckets. This framework is a permanent management lens — use it every time a new task, complaint, or process emerges.
Bucket A — Must Stay with Simon
Complex diagnostics requiring mechanical judgment and real-time problem-solving.
High-risk mechanical decisions: torque-critical work, brake system verification, electrical fault isolation.
Final pricing approval for unusual or high-ticket work outside the standard menu.
Quality control on sensitive repairs (brake comebacks, cooling system pressurization, post-starter-install verification).
Training and ride-along evaluation of new technicians.
Trust-critical customer conversations: explaining major findings, handling emotional or anxious customers, managing comeback disputes face-to-face.
Physical inspection of work performed by a second technician before sign-off.
Bucket B — Delegate to Admin / Coordinator
Appointment confirmations and day-before reminder texts.
Review request texts (sent within 10 min of job completion).
Follow-up scheduling: 48-hour check-ins, 30-day SOON reminders, 6-month MONITOR reminders.
Fleet paperwork: invoicing, PO tracking, monthly statements.
Parts ordering and will-call coordination with O’Reilly/AutoZone commercial accounts.
Bookkeeping prep: categorizing receipts, reconciling deposits, tax reserve transfers.
Basic customer communication: answering “what are your hours,” “do you work on [vehicle],” scheduling questions.
Declined-work follow-up: calling/texting customers with documented SOON items to rebook.
Google Business Profile management: posting photos, responding to reviews, updating hours.
Social media: posting before/after photos, answering community group questions.
Bucket C — Automate or AI-Assist
Missed-call auto-text: immediate text to any caller who doesn’t reach a human.
Intake pre-qualification: collecting Y/M/M, symptom, address, and urgency via text or form before human contact.
Draft estimate generation: AI drafts estimate from intake details + pricing matrix, admin reviews and sends.
Inspection summary generation: AI converts raw inspection notes/checklist into customer-friendly Now/Soon/Monitor language.
Maintenance reminder sequences: automated texts at manufacturer-recommended intervals based on last service date and mileage.
Deferred-work follow-up sequences: automated reminder at 30/60/90 days for documented SOON items.
Dashboard reporting: daily summary of jobs completed, ARO, GP/hr, travel time, review requests sent/received.
Route optimization suggestions: AI proposes next-day route order based on appointment locations.
Knowledge retrieval: searchable SOPs, torque specs, fluid capacities, and checklist access from Simon’s phone.
Bucket D — Eliminate or Redesign
Random scattered booking: customers should not be able to book at arbitrary times without route/zone awareness.
Manual re-entry of the same data: Y/M/M, address, and phone should be captured once and flow everywhere.
Undocumented verbal approvals: every approval must be written (text, email, or in-app) and logged to the system of record.
Unnecessary trips for standalone low-ticket jobs: a $40 wiper-blade-only appointment is a money-losing trip. Bundle or decline.
Ad hoc parts runs mid-day: pre-order and confirm fitment before leaving for the route. Stock the top 20 items in the van.
Unstructured post-job documentation: replace “I’ll write it up later” with a forced closeout checklist in the shop management app before leaving the site.


3. The 20 Biggest Things That Keep Simon Off a Wrench
Each blocker below is analyzed with the same structure: why it costs money, what failure looks like, who owns the fix, the exact solution, the workflow, the technology role, the solution at Day 1 / Month 6 / Month 12, and the KPI that tells you whether the system is working.
Blocker 1: Missed calls while Simon is working
Why it is expensive: Every missed call is a potential $200–$600 job lost. A customer who gets voicemail calls the next mechanic on Google. At 2–3 missed calls/day, that is $400–$1,800/day in lost opportunity.
What failure looks like: Simon stops mid-job to answer the phone, loses focus, contaminates gloves, drops a tool. Or he ignores the call and the customer is gone forever.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (primary). AI auto-text (backup).
Exact fix: Three-layer response system. Layer 1: Admin partner answers live during business hours. Layer 2: If admin misses, auto-text fires within 30 seconds: “Thanks for calling [Business]. We’re with a customer right now. Can you reply with your name, vehicle year/make/model, and what’s going on? We’ll get back to you within 15 minutes.” Layer 3: If no response to auto-text within 2 hours, admin calls back manually.
Process / workflow: Auto-text fires on missed call via OpenPhone, Halo, or similar. Text captures Y/M/M and symptom. Admin triages: qualified → schedule, unqualified → polite redirect. Result logged to system of record. Simon is NEVER interrupted unless the caller is a current-day customer with an active job question.
Technology / AI role: Day 1: OpenPhone or Google Voice with auto-text configured. Admin partner answers live. After-hours auto-text with next-business-day callback commitment.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin answers live calls + auto-text backup. Simon’s phone is on Do Not Disturb during jobs except for admin’s number.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Dedicated business line with AI voice intake (e.g., Bland.ai, Goodcall, or similar). AI captures name, Y/M/M, symptom, address, urgency. Sends structured intake summary to admin for triage. Simon never sees the intake unless admin escalates.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator owns all inbound communication. AI voice system handles overflow and after-hours. Simon is completely removed from the intake loop except for technical questions escalated by the coordinator.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Missed call rate (target: <5% during business hours). Average response time to first contact (target: <15 minutes). Calls answered by Simon per week (target: 0 by month 6).

Blocker 2: Low-quality leads and bad-fit customers
Why it is expensive: A call from someone 25 miles away wanting a transmission rebuild costs 10–15 minutes of phone time and produces zero revenue. Three of these per day = 30–45 minutes of Simon’s day wasted on work he should never do.
What failure looks like: Simon drives 40 minutes to a job site and discovers the vehicle is on a gravel slope, the customer wants a $3,000 engine swap, or the “check engine light” is actually a transmission code. The day is destroyed.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (intake screening).
Exact fix: Every lead passes through a 5-question intake screen before scheduling: (1) What’s the year, make, model, and mileage? (2) What symptom or service do you need? (3) What’s the vehicle’s address right now? (4) Is the driveway paved and relatively flat? (5) Has anyone else looked at this? If the answers reveal out-of-zone, out-of-scope, unsafe site, or transmission/engine/AC work, politely decline and refer to a shop partner.
Process / workflow: Intake form or text-based screening collects answers before booking. Admin reviews against the service-lane list, zone map, and minimum-ticket threshold. Only qualified leads reach the booking calendar. Declined leads get a referral recommendation.
Technology / AI role: AI pre-qualification via text can collect Y/M/M, symptom, and address automatically. Admin reviews the AI-collected data and makes the go/no-go decision. AI should NOT auto-book — admin confirms every booking.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin asks the 5 questions on every call. No booking without screening. Simon never talks to an unscreened lead.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: AI-assisted text intake collects structured data before admin review. Decline rate and decline reasons are tracked weekly to identify marketing misalignment.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator handles all screening. AI pre-filters obviously out-of-scope requests (transmission, engine, AC keywords) with a polite auto-redirect before a human even sees them.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Percentage of booked jobs that were pre-screened (target: 100%). Decline rate by reason (track weekly). Jobs that arrive on-site and are unworkable (target: <2% of appointments).

Blocker 3: Quote drafting and estimate writing
Why it is expensive: Writing a detailed RCW 46.71-compliant estimate from scratch takes 15–30 minutes per job. If Simon does this for 4 jobs/day, that is 1–2 hours of non-wrench time — roughly $140–$280 in lost production daily.
What failure looks like: Simon is sitting in the van between jobs writing estimates on his phone, or he’s doing it at 8 PM after a 10-hour day. Estimates go out late, customers book elsewhere, and Simon burns out.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (draft and send). Simon (approve unusual estimates only).
Exact fix: Pre-built estimate templates for every service in the menu (oil change, brake service, battery, belt, thermostat, etc.) stored in the shop management system. For standard work, admin selects the template, adjusts for vehicle class, and sends. For non-standard work, Simon provides a 30-second voice note (“2019 CR-V, front brakes, pads and rotors, one caliper slide pin boot, about 1.5 hours”), and admin converts it into a formatted estimate.
Process / workflow: Simon diagnoses → sends voice note or checklist to admin → admin drafts estimate from template + voice note → estimate sent to customer within 30 minutes of diagnosis → customer approves via text/email link → approval logged to system of record.
Technology / AI role: AI can convert Simon’s voice notes into structured estimate drafts using speech-to-text + template matching. Admin reviews the AI draft for accuracy, adjusts pricing if needed, and sends. AI should NOT send estimates without human review.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Pre-built templates in AutoLeap/Tekmetric for top 20 services. Admin sends all standard estimates. Simon only writes estimates for unusual/complex work.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: AI draft estimates from voice notes. Admin reviews and sends within 15 minutes. Estimate turnaround tracked as a KPI.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator drafts and sends all estimates. AI handles first-draft generation. Simon’s involvement limited to approving estimates over a defined dollar threshold (e.g., $500+) or for non-standard work.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Estimate turnaround time (target: <30 minutes from diagnosis to customer receipt). Estimates drafted by Simon per week (target: 0 standard estimates by month 6). Quote-to-close conversion rate (target: 60%+).

Blocker 4: Approval chasing and customer indecision
Why it is expensive: A customer who takes 2 days to approve a $400 brake job ties up a calendar slot, delays parts ordering, and forces Simon to context-switch when the approval finally comes. If 3 approvals per week are slow, that is scheduling chaos.
What failure looks like: Simon calls the customer twice, texts once, and then the customer ghosts. The job dies. Or the customer approves at 4 PM Friday and expects the work done Saturday morning — parts aren’t ordered and Simon’s weekend is hijacked.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (follow-up). System (auto-reminders).
Exact fix: Estimates include a clear expiration window (48–72 hours). Auto-reminder fires at 24 hours if not approved. Admin follows up with a phone call at 48 hours. If no response by 72 hours, the estimate expires and the calendar slot is released. Re-quoting requires a new appointment.
Process / workflow: Estimate sent with approval link → 24-hour auto-reminder → 48-hour admin call → 72-hour expiration → slot released. Customer can re-request at any time but goes back into the queue.
Technology / AI role: Automated approval reminders via shop management system or text sequence. Admin monitors approval dashboard daily and manually follows up on high-value pending approvals.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin sends estimates with “This quote is valid for 72 hours” language. Admin sets phone reminders to follow up.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Auto-reminder sequence at 24/48/72 hours built into the shop management system or a text automation tool (e.g., Podium, Broadly, or custom Zapier flow). Admin only handles manual escalation.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator owns the approval pipeline. Pending approvals are visible on the daily dashboard. Auto-expiration is enforced consistently. Simon is never involved in chasing approvals.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Approval turnaround time (target: <24 hours for 70%+ of estimates). Estimate expiration rate (track — high expiration may indicate pricing or communication issue). Approvals chased by Simon per week (target: 0).

Blocker 5: Weak calendar design and schedule chaos
Why it is expensive: A poorly designed daily schedule — wrong job sequence, too many jobs, insufficient buffers, geographic scatter — turns a 5-billable-hour day into a 3-billable-hour day. That’s $280 in lost production per bad day.
What failure looks like: Simon arrives at Job 3 and realizes he needs to drive 25 minutes back to the zone he left 2 hours ago for Job 5. Or he has no buffer between a brake job and an oil change and is 45 minutes late to every afternoon appointment. Customers notice.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (schedule design). Simon (input on job sequencing for complex work).
Exact fix: The daily schedule is designed by the admin partner the evening before, using these rules: (1) Jobs are sequenced by geography, not by booking order. (2) 15-minute buffers between jobs. (3) No more than 4–5 appointments on a standard day. (4) Complex/uncertain jobs go first or last, never mid-day. (5) Lunch break is blocked, not squeezed out. (6) The schedule is confirmed by 7 PM the night before and texted to Simon.
Process / workflow: Admin builds tomorrow’s route by 7 PM tonight → confirms all customers via text → pre-orders parts for morning pickup → texts Simon the final schedule by 7 PM → Simon’s only morning task is to pick up parts and drive to Job 1.
Technology / AI role: Route optimization tools (OptimoRoute, Routific, or even Google Maps multi-stop). AI can suggest optimal job order based on addresses. Admin reviews and adjusts for job complexity and customer preferences.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin builds the next day’s schedule manually using Google Maps multi-stop. Simon gets a text with the job list, addresses, and times by 7 PM.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Route optimization software suggests daily order. Admin reviews and sends. Customer confirmations are automated the evening before. Parts pre-staging is integrated into the scheduling workflow.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator owns the calendar entirely. Route optimization is standard. Simon receives his daily schedule like an employee would — show up, wrench, go home.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Average drive time between jobs (target: <20 minutes). Schedule changes after 7 PM the night before (target: <1 per week). Jobs per day completed vs. planned (target: 90%+ completion rate). Customer complaints about lateness (target: 0).

Blocker 6: Route planning and drive-time waste
Why it is expensive: Every minute driving between jobs is a minute Simon is not billing. At $140/hr, 30 minutes of excess drive time per day costs $70/day = $17,500/year. Route discipline is not a convenience — it is a revenue protection system.
What failure looks like: Simon drives from Spokane Valley to the South Hill to North Spokane to Airway Heights in one day because jobs were booked by time-of-call, not by geography. He spends 2.5 hours driving and 5 hours wrenching instead of 1.5 hours driving and 6 hours wrenching.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (route design). Zone rules (structural).
Exact fix: Zone-day discipline: assign specific days or half-days to specific geographic zones. Monday = Spokane Valley. Tuesday = South Hill. Wednesday = North Spokane. Thursday = flex/overflow. Friday = fleet/commercial blocks. Customers in the wrong zone for a given day are offered the next available zone-day slot, not squeezed in randomly.
Process / workflow: Customer calls for Spokane Valley service → admin checks zone calendar → if Monday is Spokane Valley day and it’s available, book it → if Monday is full, offer next Monday or Thursday flex → customer never knows the internal system, they just get a date that works.
Technology / AI role: Route optimization software proposes efficient daily ordering. GPS tracking (on second van) validates route compliance. Weekly review of average drive time identifies zone-day effectiveness.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: One tight zone only. No countywide booking. Admin manually ensures geographic clustering.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Zone-day system established. Admin uses route software to optimize within zones. Average drive time tracked weekly.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Two routes (owner + tech) with zone assignments. Coordinator manages both calendars with route optimization. Drive time is a KPI on the weekly dashboard.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Average drive time between jobs (target: <20 min in zone, <25 min overall). Percentage of jobs booked in the correct zone-day (target: 85%+). Revenue per route hour (target: $120+ GP/hr).

Blocker 7: Parts research and fitment verification
Why it is expensive: Looking up the correct part number, verifying fitment for a specific Y/M/M/engine, and comparing prices across suppliers takes 10–20 minutes per job. If Simon does this for 4 jobs, that is 40–80 minutes of non-wrench time daily.
What failure looks like: Simon orders the wrong brake pads (2019 CR-V pads don’t fit a 2020 CR-V because Honda changed the caliper design). He discovers this on-site. Now he’s making a parts run, the customer is waiting, and the schedule is destroyed.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (research and ordering). Simon (confirmation on unusual parts only).
Exact fix: Admin researches and verifies parts the evening before or morning of the appointment. Uses the system of record’s vehicle profile (Y/M/M/engine) to pull correct part numbers from O’Reilly/AutoZone online catalogs. Cross-references with the intake notes. Places will-call orders by 7 AM for morning pickup. Simon’s only involvement: confirming unusual or ambiguous parts (e.g., “this truck has the heavy-duty towing package — make sure it’s the HD caliper”).
Process / workflow: Admin receives tomorrow’s schedule → looks up each job’s vehicle and service → researches correct parts → places will-call orders → confirms availability and pickup location → includes parts list in Simon’s daily schedule text.
Technology / AI role: AI can assist by auto-suggesting part numbers from Y/M/M + service type using catalog APIs. Admin verifies. AI should NOT place orders autonomously — fitment errors are too costly.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin manually researches parts using AutoZone.com and OReilly.com part lookup tools. Places will-call orders by phone or online. Confirms availability before booking.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Parts research integrated into the scheduling workflow. Admin uses catalog lookup tools with saved vehicle profiles. Common parts for top 20 vehicles are pre-identified and stocked in the van.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator handles all parts research and ordering. Van stock is managed with a restock checklist. AI suggests part numbers; coordinator verifies and orders. Wrong-part rate is tracked as a quality metric.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Parts pre-staged before route departure (target: 100% of standard jobs). Wrong-part events per month (target: <1). Mid-day parts runs per week (target: <1). Parts research minutes performed by Simon per week (target: 0).

Blocker 8: Parts ordering and will-call coordination
Why it is expensive: Calling a parts store, waiting on hold, confirming availability, placing the order, and coordinating pickup timing takes 10–15 minutes per order. If Simon does this 3 times per day, that is 30–45 minutes of lost wrench time.
What failure looks like: Simon calls O’Reilly at 7:30 AM but the part isn’t in stock at the nearest location. He calls three more stores. By the time he finds it, he’s 30 minutes behind schedule and his first customer is texting “are you still coming?”
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (all ordering).
Exact fix: Admin places all parts orders the evening before. Uses commercial account online ordering for will-call pickup. Confirms stock availability before placing the order. If the primary store is out, checks the secondary store. If both are out, adjusts the schedule or reschedules the job BEFORE Simon’s day starts.
Process / workflow: Admin builds parts list from tomorrow’s schedule → checks availability online → places will-call orders → confirms pickup window → includes pickup instructions in Simon’s daily text. Simon’s morning: pick up parts at one stop, drive to Job 1.
Technology / AI role: Online will-call ordering via O’Reilly.com and AutoZone.com commercial accounts. No phone ordering unless the online system is down.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin places all will-call orders online the night before. Simon picks up in the morning. No mid-day ordering unless a same-day discovery requires it.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Parts ordering fully systematized. Van stock of top 20 consumables reduces ordering frequency by 30–40%. Restock checklist run weekly.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator owns all parts logistics. Van stock managed with a minimum-quantity reorder system. AI can flag when common parts are running low based on job history.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Parts pre-ordered vs. same-day orders (target: 90%+ pre-ordered). Parts pickup stops per morning (target: 1). Simon time spent on parts coordination per week (target: 0 minutes).

Blocker 9: Van inventory disorder and stockouts
Why it is expensive: A disorganized van wastes 5–10 minutes per job searching for tools, parts, and supplies. A stockout (out of brake cleaner, out of drain plug gaskets, out of shop towels) forces a mid-day store run that costs 30–45 minutes.
What failure looks like: Simon reaches for a 15mm deep socket and it’s not in the drawer. He spends 5 minutes finding it under the creeper. Then he discovers he’s out of copper drain plug gaskets. Now he’s making a run to AutoZone for a $3 part, costing 30 minutes of billable time.
Who owns the fix: Simon (van organization). Admin (restock system).
Exact fix: Van organization follows a fixed layout: every tool, part bin, and fluid container has a designated location. A shadow board or labeled shelf system ensures nothing drifts. Weekly restock checklist (run every Friday or Saturday) covers: brake cleaner, shop towels, gloves, drain plug gaskets, common filters, PB Blaster, brake fluid, coolant, fuses, and the top 10 oil filters.
Process / workflow: Friday afternoon: Simon runs the restock checklist against van inventory → texts the restock list to admin → admin orders or picks up Saturday morning → van is fully stocked before Monday.
Technology / AI role: AI can generate a suggested restock list based on next week’s scheduled jobs (e.g., 3 oil changes = confirm 3 filters + 3 drain gaskets in stock). Admin verifies and adjusts.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Fixed van layout from launch. Written inventory list taped inside van door. Manual restock check every Friday.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Digital restock checklist in a shared note or task app. Admin manages restocking. Van stock adjusted based on actual usage patterns from the first 6 months.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator manages restock. Minimum-quantity alerts for critical items. Van organization audited monthly.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Mid-day store runs per week (target: <1). Time-to-find-tool average (informal but real — if Simon is frequently searching, the layout needs fixing). Stockout events per month (target: 0 for critical items like brake cleaner, drain gaskets, and gloves).

Blocker 10: Unstructured inspection notes
Why it is expensive: If Simon’s inspection findings live in his head, on a scrap of paper, or in a disorganized text to himself, they cannot be converted into customer-facing recommendations, deferred-work records, or follow-up reminders. The entire revenue maximization engine (Section 9 of the Investor Plan) depends on structured, searchable inspection data.
What failure looks like: Simon finishes an oil change, tells the customer “your brakes are getting thin and your cabin filter is dirty,” the customer says “not today,” and Simon drives away. That finding is never documented. The 30-day follow-up never happens. The $280 brake job is lost.
Who owns the fix: Simon (capture findings during inspection). Admin/AI (convert into customer language and deferred-work records).
Exact fix: Use the shop management system’s digital inspection checklist on every job. Simon taps through the checklist on his phone or tablet: battery (pass/fail/weak), brakes (mm measurement), tires (tread depth), filters (good/dirty/replace), fluids (level/condition), belts (condition), lights (all working Y/N). Photos attached for any finding that needs customer attention. Results saved to the vehicle record automatically.
Process / workflow: Simon performs inspection → taps through digital checklist → attaches photos → results auto-save to vehicle record → admin/AI converts findings into customer-facing Now/Soon/Monitor summary → summary texted or emailed to customer within 1 hour → declined items logged as deferred work with follow-up date.
Technology / AI role: Digital vehicle inspection (DVI) built into AutoLeap, Tekmetric, or equivalent. AI can convert checklist results into customer-friendly language (e.g., “Brake pads at 3mm — recommend replacement within 60 days” becomes “Your front brake pads are getting low. They’re safe for now but should be replaced in the next month or two to avoid noise and rotor damage.”).
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Use the shop management system’s built-in DVI checklist. Simon completes it on every job. Admin reviews and sends the summary to the customer.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: AI generates customer-facing summaries from inspection data. Admin reviews before sending. Deferred-work items auto-populate a follow-up queue with reminder dates.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: DVI completion is mandatory for job closeout — the invoice cannot be generated until the inspection is complete. Coordinator manages the deferred-work pipeline. AI handles reminder sequences.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: DVI completion rate (target: 100% of maintenance visits). Deferred-work items logged per week (track volume). Deferred-work conversion rate within 90 days (target: 25–40%). Inspection summaries sent to customers within 1 hour of job completion (target: 95%+).


Blocker 11: Turning findings into customer language
Why it is expensive: Raw inspection data (“front pads 3mm, RF caliper slide pin dry, brake fluid 4.2% moisture”) means nothing to a customer. Someone must translate it into language the customer can understand and act on. If Simon does this verbally for every customer, it takes 5–10 minutes per job and the translation is inconsistent.
What failure looks like: Simon tells one customer “your brakes are thin” and another customer “your front brake pad friction material is approaching minimum thickness specification.” Neither customer knows what to do. The message doesn’t convert into a booking.
Who owns the fix: AI (draft translation). Admin (review and send).
Exact fix: AI converts structured DVI checklist data into standardized customer-friendly language using pre-built templates. For each finding category (brakes, battery, filters, fluids, belts, tires), there is a template that converts the measurement/condition into: (1) plain-language description, (2) urgency classification (Now/Soon/Monitor), and (3) a recommended action with approximate price range.
Process / workflow: DVI checklist completed by Simon → AI generates customer summary from templates → admin reviews for accuracy and tone → summary sent to customer via text/email → customer responds or admin follows up.
Technology / AI role: AI language generation from structured data (GPT-based or template-based). This is one of the highest-leverage AI applications in the business because it converts mechanical data into revenue-generating communication without requiring Simon’s time.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin manually writes customer summaries from Simon’s checklist data. Use pre-written template phrases for common findings.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: AI drafts summaries. Admin reviews and sends. Turnaround time drops from 30+ minutes to 5–10 minutes.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: AI drafts, coordinator reviews and sends. Summary quality is audited monthly for tone and accuracy.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Time from job completion to customer summary delivery (target: <1 hour). Summaries sent per completed job (target: 100% of maintenance visits). Customer response rate to summaries (track — indicates clarity and relevance).

Blocker 12: Photo and evidence management
Why it is expensive: Inspection photos are worthless if they are trapped in Simon’s phone camera roll, unsearchable, unnamed, and disconnected from the vehicle record. The entire evidence-based recommendation system requires photos to be attached to specific findings on specific vehicles.
What failure looks like: Simon takes 6 photos during a brake inspection but they live in his personal camera roll. When the customer calls 3 weeks later asking “how bad were my brakes again?” nobody can find the photos. The deferred-work follow-up has no evidence to reference. Trust erodes.
Who owns the fix: Simon (capture photos through the DVI app, not personal camera). System of record (storage and retrieval).
Exact fix: All inspection photos must be taken THROUGH the shop management app’s DVI module, not through the phone’s native camera. This ensures every photo is automatically attached to the correct vehicle record, tagged with the date and finding category, and searchable later.
Process / workflow: Simon opens DVI checklist for brake inspection → takes photo of pad thickness through the app → photo attaches to “Front brakes” finding → photo is visible to admin and customer when the summary is sent → photo is permanently stored in the vehicle’s service history.
Technology / AI role: Shop management DVI module (AutoLeap, Tekmetric, Shop-Ware). No additional AI needed for basic photo management — the tool handles it. AI can assist by auto-categorizing photos or flagging missing photo fields on the checklist.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Simon uses DVI app for all inspection photos. No personal camera roll photos for work purposes. Admin enforces this rule.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Photo completion audited weekly. Any DVI submitted without photos for flagged findings is returned to Simon for completion before invoicing.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Photo protocol is standard for both routes. Coordinator audits technician photos for quality and completeness.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Percentage of DVI checklists with photos attached (target: 100% for any finding rated SOON or NOW). Customer reference to photos in reviews or follow-up (qualitative tracking). Photo storage searchability — can admin find any vehicle’s last inspection photos within 60 seconds? (target: yes, always).

Blocker 13: Invoicing and payment collection
Why it is expensive: Generating an invoice, collecting payment, and reconciling the transaction takes 5–10 minutes per job. If Simon does this while the customer watches, it’s 5 minutes he’s not already en route to the next job. If he does it later, the delay creates collection risk.
What failure looks like: Simon finishes a brake job, spends 8 minutes fumbling with the invoicing app on his phone while the customer stands in the driveway. Or worse, he says “I’ll send you the invoice later” and the customer forgets, disputes the amount, or never pays.
Who owns the fix: Simon (collect payment on-site via tap-to-pay). Admin (generate final invoice from work completed).
Exact fix: Simon carries Square tap-to-pay. At job completion, admin generates the final invoice in the shop management system (from the estimate + any approved additional work). Invoice is texted to the customer while Simon does the post-job walkthrough. Customer pays via tap-to-pay or the invoice link. Payment is confirmed before Simon leaves the site.
Process / workflow: Simon completes work → texts admin “Job done, [any additions]” → admin generates invoice in shop management system → invoice texted to customer → Simon does walkthrough and collects tap-to-pay → payment confirmed → Simon drives to next job.
Technology / AI role: Shop management system handles invoice generation. Square/Stripe handles tap-to-pay. No AI needed for basic invoicing — but AI can auto-populate the invoice from the completed DVI and repair order data.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Simon collects payment on-site via Square tap-to-pay. Admin generates final invoices within 15 minutes of job completion.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Invoicing is near-instant — admin generates from completed repair order data. Payment is collected before departure on 95%+ of retail jobs.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator generates all invoices. Fleet invoices are batched monthly. Payment collection discipline is enforced: no work without payment unless the customer is on an approved fleet net-30 account.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Payment collected on-site rate (target: 95%+ retail). Time from job completion to invoice sent (target: <15 minutes). Outstanding receivables over 30 days (target: <$500 total). Invoicing minutes performed by Simon (target: 0 by month 6).

Blocker 14: Review requests and reputation capture
Why it is expensive: Every missed review request is a missed compounding trust asset. At 10% organic review rate, you get 1 review per 10 customers. With a systematic ask, you get 1 per 3–4 customers. Over a year, that is the difference between 30 reviews and 100+ reviews — which directly affects Google ranking, close rates, and paid acquisition cost.
What failure looks like: Simon finishes a great job but forgets to ask for a review. Or he asks verbally and the customer says “sure” and never does it. The business has 15 reviews at month 6 instead of 50, and Google Ads CPL is 2× higher than projected.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (automated). Simon (verbal ask during walkthrough — optional but helpful).
Exact fix: Automated text sent within 10 minutes of job completion: “Hi [Name], thanks for choosing [Business]! If you were happy with the service, a Google review would mean the world: [direct review link]. Thanks! — Simon.” If no response within 48 hours, one follow-up text. Never more than two asks.
Process / workflow: Job marked complete in system of record → auto-text fires with review link → if no review within 48 hours, follow-up text fires → no further asks → review count tracked weekly on dashboard.
Technology / AI role: Text automation via Podium, Broadly, or the shop management system’s built-in review request feature. Direct Google review link shortcut (generated from Google Business Profile).
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin manually texts review request within 10 minutes of every completed job. Uses a pre-written template with the direct review link.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Automated review request triggered by job completion in the system of record. Admin monitors review conversion rate weekly.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Fully automated. Coordinator monitors review volume and sentiment. Review response to every review within 24 hours.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Review requests sent per completed job (target: 100%). Review conversion rate (target: 25–35% of asks result in a review). Monthly new reviews (target: 8–12 by month 6, 15+ by month 12). Average review score (target: 4.8+). Reviews responded to within 24 hours (target: 100%).

Blocker 15: Deferred-work follow-up
Why it is expensive: Every SOON item that goes unfollowed-up is a pre-sold job that died in the pipeline. If the average SOON item is worth $250 and you lose 3 per week to lack of follow-up, that is $750/week = $37,500/year in abandoned revenue from work the customer already knows they need.
What failure looks like: Simon documented that a customer’s coolant was dark and overdue for a flush. The finding was noted in the DVI. Nobody followed up. Eight months later the customer goes to Jiffy Lube and gets the flush there. The customer is still yours for oil changes but you lost a $150 high-margin job.
Who owns the fix: Admin/coordinator (follow-up execution). AI (reminder triggers).
Exact fix: Every SOON finding is logged with: vehicle, finding description, estimated cost, urgency, and target follow-up date (typically 30 days for SOON, 180 days for MONITOR). At the follow-up date, the system generates a reminder: “Hi [Name], when we serviced your [Vehicle] last month, we noted your [finding]. Ready to get that taken care of? I have openings this week.”
Process / workflow: SOON finding documented in DVI → deferred-work record created with follow-up date → at follow-up date, auto-reminder text sent → if customer responds, admin books the appointment → if no response within 7 days, one more follow-up → if still no response, record is marked dormant and moves to 6-month MONITOR cycle.
Technology / AI role: Shop management system deferred-work tracking. Text automation for follow-up reminders. AI can prioritize the deferred-work queue by dollar value, vehicle age, and likelihood of conversion.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin maintains a simple spreadsheet of deferred work: customer name, vehicle, finding, $ estimate, follow-up date. Admin manually texts follow-ups weekly.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Deferred work tracked in the shop management system. Auto-reminders fire at 30/60/90 days. Admin reviews the pipeline weekly and prioritizes high-value follow-ups.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator owns the deferred-work pipeline. AI generates prioritized follow-up lists weekly. Conversion rate tracked as a KPI on the dashboard.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Deferred-work items created per week (track volume). Follow-up texts sent on schedule (target: 100%). Deferred-work conversion rate within 90 days (target: 25–40%). Total $ in active deferred-work pipeline (track — this is your pre-sold future revenue).

Blocker 16: Maintenance reminders and repeat booking
Why it is expensive: A customer who got an oil change 5 months ago is due for another one. If nobody reminds them, they go to Jiffy Lube or forget until their oil light comes on. That is a $110+ recurring revenue event lost to silence. At 200 oil-change customers per year, a 10% reminder failure rate = 20 lost repeat visits = $2,200+ in direct revenue plus downstream deferred-work opportunity.
What failure looks like: The business has 150 customers in the database but no systematic reminder process. Repeat visit rate is 25% instead of 40%+ because customers simply forget.
Who owns the fix: Admin/coordinator (execution). System (triggers based on service date and mileage estimate).
Exact fix: At every oil change, the system records: service date, odometer reading, and next recommended service date (calculated at 5,000–7,500 miles or 6 months, whichever comes first). At the reminder date, an auto-text fires: “Hi [Name], your [Vehicle] is coming due for its next oil service based on our last visit [X months ago]. Want to schedule? Reply YES and we’ll find a time that works.”
Process / workflow: Oil change completed → next service date calculated and stored → at reminder date, auto-text fires → customer replies YES → admin/coordinator books the appointment → customer replies NO or doesn’t respond → second reminder at next month → after two unanswered reminders, move to semi-annual reminder cycle.
Technology / AI role: Maintenance reminder automation built into the shop management system or a standalone text automation tool. Calendar-based triggers. AI can adjust reminder timing based on estimated driving patterns (e.g., a customer who drives 15K miles/year gets reminded sooner than a customer who drives 8K).
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin manually tracks next-service dates in a spreadsheet or the shop management system. Sends reminder texts manually at the appropriate interval.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Auto-reminders configured in the shop management system. Admin monitors booking responses. Repeat visit rate tracked monthly.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Fully automated. Coordinator handles exceptions (customers who never respond, customers who moved away, customers who sold the vehicle). AI adjusts timing based on customer driving patterns.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Maintenance reminders sent on schedule (target: 100%). Repeat visit rate within 12 months (target: 35–50% of oil change customers return). Reminder-to-booking conversion rate (target: 20–30%). Customers who have not been contacted in 6+ months (target: 0 — everyone gets at least a semi-annual check-in).

Blocker 17: Bookkeeping, receipts, and admin cleanup
Why it is expensive: Unsorted receipts, unreconciled deposits, missed tax reserve transfers, and end-of-month bookkeeping panic steal 3–5 hours per week from someone. If Simon does it, that is $420–$700/week in lost wrench time. If admin does it but inefficiently, it creates errors that cost more to fix later.
What failure looks like: Tax season arrives and there are 47 uncategorized receipts, 3 months of unreconciled bank statements, and no clear record of quarterly estimated tax payments. The CPA charges $1,500 extra to sort the mess, and the business discovers it owes $4,000 more than expected.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (100% ownership from day 1). Simon’s involvement: zero.
Exact fix: QuickBooks Self-Employed or QuickBooks Online for daily categorization. Every receipt photographed and uploaded same-day via the mobile app. Bank feed connected for automatic transaction import. Tax reserve account: 28% of every deposit auto-transferred. Quarterly estimated tax payments made on schedule (April 15, June 15, Sept 15, Jan 15). Weekly 30-minute bookkeeping review by admin.
Process / workflow: Daily: admin photographs and categorizes receipts. Weekly: admin reviews QuickBooks dashboard, reconciles any unmatched transactions, verifies tax reserve balance. Monthly: admin generates P&L summary and reviews against plan. Quarterly: admin ensures estimated tax payment is made on time.
Technology / AI role: QuickBooks mobile app with receipt scanning and auto-categorization. Bank feed integration. Mileage tracking via QuickBooks or a dedicated app. AI can flag unusual expenses or categorization errors.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin sets up QuickBooks, connects bank account, creates tax reserve savings account, and establishes the receipt-capture habit. Simon never touches bookkeeping.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Bookkeeping is routine. Admin spends 30 minutes/week on it. Quarterly taxes are on autopilot. Monthly P&L review is standard.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator or admin handles all bookkeeping. CPA engagement is minimal because records are clean year-round. Year-end filing is straightforward.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Uncategorized receipts older than 7 days (target: 0). Tax reserve balance vs. target (28% of gross deposits — checked weekly). Quarterly estimated tax payments made on time (target: 100%). Monthly P&L generated by the 5th of the following month (target: yes, every month).

Blocker 18: Warranty and comeback triage
Why it is expensive: A customer calls 10 days after a brake job and says the brakes are squealing. Is this a legitimate comeback (workmanship issue), a break-in period squeal (normal for new ceramic pads), or a new problem unrelated to the repair? If Simon handles this triage call, that is 15–30 minutes of phone time plus potential emotional stress. If nobody handles it well, a 1-star review follows.
What failure looks like: Customer calls and says “you broke my car.” Simon gets defensive. The customer leaves a 2-star review saying “they were rude when I called about a problem.” Three potential referrals die with that review.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (first contact and triage). Simon (technical evaluation if re-inspection is needed).
Exact fix: Admin answers the callback with empathy and a standardized script: “I’m sorry to hear that. Let me pull up your vehicle record and see exactly what was done. Can you describe what you’re experiencing?” Admin reviews the repair order, DVI photos, and invoice. Classifies the issue as: (A) likely related to recent work → schedule free re-inspection within 24–48 hours, (B) possibly unrelated → schedule a diagnostic appointment (may be charged), or (C) normal break-in behavior → explain, reassure, and offer a free check at 500 miles if they’re still concerned.
Process / workflow: Customer calls with complaint → admin answers with script → admin pulls vehicle record → admin classifies issue (A/B/C) → schedules appropriate response → Simon performs re-inspection only if classified A or B → outcome documented in vehicle record → admin follows up after resolution.
Technology / AI role: System of record provides instant access to the original repair order, parts used, photos taken, and warranty terms. AI can assist by surfacing the original job details when the customer’s phone number is recognized.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin handles all comeback/warranty calls. Uses the standardized script. Simon does NOT answer comeback calls directly. All comebacks documented.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Comeback handling is systematized. Every callback is logged with: customer, vehicle, original job, complaint, classification (A/B/C), resolution, cost. Comeback rate tracked as a KPI.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator owns all warranty triage. Technician comebacks on Route 2 are tracked separately. Comeback trends are reviewed monthly to identify process improvements.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Comeback rate (target: <2% of jobs). Comebacks handled by Simon vs. admin (target: Simon only handles the re-inspection, never the phone call). Average comeback resolution time (target: <48 hours from complaint to re-inspection). Reviews mentioning comebacks (track sentiment — a comeback handled well can still produce a 5-star review).

Blocker 19: Fleet paperwork, collections, and account handling
Why it is expensive: Fleet accounts generate recurring revenue but also generate recurring admin: PO numbers, monthly invoices, vehicle-specific service tracking, aging receivables, and fleet manager communication. If Simon handles any of this, it is the worst possible use of his time.
What failure looks like: Simon finishes servicing 3 trucks for a landscaping company. He forgets to note the PO number on the work order. The fleet manager disputes the invoice 45 days later because “it wasn’t authorized under our PO system.” $1,200 in receivables is at risk.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (100% ownership). Simon’s involvement: zero on paperwork, limited to technical questions from fleet managers.
Exact fix: Every fleet account has a documented profile: company name, fleet manager contact, billing address, PO requirement (Y/N), payment terms, vehicle list with VINs, service schedule, and account notes. Admin generates fleet invoices monthly (or per the agreed billing cycle). PO numbers are captured at the time of service authorization, not after the fact. Admin monitors fleet receivables weekly — any invoice over 30 days gets a follow-up call.
Process / workflow: Fleet job booked → admin confirms PO number before the appointment → Simon performs the work → admin generates invoice with PO reference → invoice sent to fleet manager on billing cycle → admin monitors aging → follow-up at 30 days → escalation at 45 days.
Technology / AI role: Shop management system fleet module for vehicle-level tracking and account-level invoicing. Accounts receivable aging report generated weekly. No AI needed for basic fleet admin — this is process discipline.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Admin maintains fleet accounts in the shop management system. Captures PO numbers at booking. Generates invoices on schedule. Simon never touches fleet paperwork.
Month 6 solution: Month 6: Fleet billing is standardized. Monthly statements sent automatically. Aging tracked on the weekly dashboard.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: Coordinator owns fleet administration. Fleet accounts have annual reviews for pricing, service scope, and payment performance. Problem accounts are flagged and addressed proactively.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Fleet invoices sent on schedule (target: 100%). Fleet receivables over 30 days (target: <$1,000 total). PO capture rate at time of booking (target: 100% for PO-required accounts). Simon time spent on fleet admin per week (target: 0).

Blocker 20: Fragmented systems and multiple sources of truth
Why it is expensive: When customer data lives in texts, vehicle info lives in a spreadsheet, estimates live in email, photos live in a camera roll, invoices live in Square, and deferred work lives in Simon’s head, the business cannot scale, cannot defend a dispute, cannot follow up reliably, and cannot train a new employee.
What failure looks like: A new coordinator starts in month 10. She asks “where is Mr. Johnson’s last service record?” The answer is: “Simon texted the findings to me, I drafted the estimate in email, the photos are on Simon’s phone, the invoice is in Square, and the deferred work is... somewhere.” Training this coordinator takes 3 weeks instead of 3 days because the system is a person, not a process.
Who owns the fix: Admin partner (system architecture from day 1). Entire team (discipline to use one system).
Exact fix: ONE system of record. Every estimate, every inspection, every photo, every approval, every invoice, every deferred-work note, every customer interaction, and every vehicle record lives in the shop management system (AutoLeap, Tekmetric, or equivalent). Nothing important lives only in texts, emails, personal notes, spreadsheets, or camera rolls. If it didn’t go into the system, it didn’t happen.
Process / workflow: Every workflow ends with data going into the system of record: intake → system. Estimate → system. Inspection → system. Photos → system. Approval → system. Invoice → system. Payment → system. Deferred work → system. Follow-up status → system.
Technology / AI role: The system of record IS the technology solution for this blocker. Additional tools (texting, route planning, bookkeeping) connect to it or export from it, but the system of record is the canonical source of truth. AI and automation layers pull from and push to the system of record, never replace it.
Day 1 solution: Day 1: Choose one shop management system. Commit to it. Enter every job from day one. No exceptions. No “I’ll put it in later.”
Month 6 solution: Month 6: The system of record contains a complete history of every customer, vehicle, job, finding, approval, and invoice. Admin can answer any question about any past job within 60 seconds.
Month 12 solution: Month 12: New coordinator and technician onboard using the system of record as their training platform. Every workflow is documented in the system. The business is transferable because the knowledge lives in the system, not in Simon’s head.
KPI / warning sign: KPI: Percentage of jobs with complete records in the system of record (target: 100%). Time to answer “what did we do for [customer] last time?” (target: <60 seconds). Number of tools where customer data lives (target: 1 primary system of record + connected tools, never standalone silos).


4. Frontier AI and Automation Architecture
AI is NOT being used to replace Simon’s mechanical judgment. It is being used to protect wrench time by removing admin and coordination drag. Every AI application listed below is evaluated against one question: does this give Simon back a wrench-minute?
4A. Voice and Intake Automation
Missed-call auto-text: fires within 30 seconds of any unanswered call. Captures name, Y/M/M, symptom, address via text conversation.
After-hours AI voice intake (Month 6+): AI answers, collects structured intake data, sends summary to admin for next-business-day triage. Does NOT schedule appointments or make commitments.
Pre-qualification filtering: AI flags obviously out-of-scope requests (transmission, engine, AC keywords) with a polite auto-redirect before admin sees them.
Urgent vs. non-urgent routing: AI asks “is the vehicle currently undrivable?” to prioritize battery/no-start calls over routine maintenance requests.
4B. AI-Assisted Estimating and Approvals
Draft estimates: AI generates estimate from intake data + service menu + vehicle-class pricing matrix. Admin reviews accuracy, adjusts if needed, and sends.
Voice-to-estimate: Simon leaves a 30-second voice note (“2019 CR-V, front brakes, pads and rotors, about 1.5 hours”). AI transcribes and drafts a formatted estimate. Admin reviews and sends.
Approval follow-up: automated reminder sequence at 24/48/72 hours for pending estimates. Admin manually escalates high-value estimates.
4C. AI-Assisted Inspections and Communication
Finding translation: AI converts DVI checklist data into customer-friendly Now/Soon/Monitor summaries. Admin reviews tone and accuracy before sending.
Photo integration: AI can auto-caption inspection photos based on the checklist category they were uploaded under.
Deferred-work follow-up drafting: AI generates personalized follow-up messages based on the specific finding, vehicle, and time elapsed.
Review request messages: AI personalizes the review request based on the service performed (“Thanks for trusting us with your brake service today” vs. generic “thanks for your business”).
4D. AI-Assisted Operations and Reporting
Daily schedule summaries: AI compiles tomorrow’s route with job details, parts status, and customer notes into one morning briefing text for Simon.
Route optimization: AI suggests optimal job sequence based on addresses and estimated job duration.
KPI reporting: AI generates weekly dashboard from system-of-record data. Highlights jobs below target GP/hr, customers due for service, and unresolved deferred work.
Fleet maintenance reporting: AI generates monthly fleet service summaries for fleet managers from individual vehicle service records.
4E. AI Knowledge and SOP Layer
Searchable SOPs: all operating procedures, checklists, and scripts accessible from Simon’s phone via a simple search interface.
Torque spec and fluid capacity lookup: AI provides instant answers to “what’s the lug nut torque on a 2022 CR-V?” or “how much coolant does a 7.3L F-250 take?”
Training support: structured onboarding content for new technicians, searchable and referenceable on the job.
Scripting support: pre-built customer communication scripts for common scenarios (comeback call, declined-work follow-up, fleet pitch) accessible from phone.


5. What Frontier AI Should NOT Do
These guardrails protect the business from the risks of over-automation. They are as important as the automation itself.
AI should NOT independently approve repairs or authorize work. All repair approvals require customer authorization documented in the system of record.
AI should NOT commit to prices or scope on unusual/non-standard work without human review. Standard menu pricing is fine; custom quotes require admin or Simon approval.
AI should NOT replace Simon’s judgment on complex diagnostics. AI can suggest differential diagnoses and surface relevant TSBs, but the technician makes the call.
AI should NOT become the legal system of record by itself. All customer-facing commitments, authorizations, and warranty terms must live in the shop management system, not in an AI chat log.
AI should NOT message customers with unlimited autonomy. All AI-generated customer communications are reviewed by a human before sending (admin on Day 1, coordinator by Month 12) unless the message is a simple pre-approved template (review request, appointment confirmation).
AI should NOT replace proper documentation, photo evidence, or inspection checklists. AI enhances the presentation of this data; it does not substitute for capturing it.
AI should NOT create software complexity faster than operational maturity. If the business hasn’t proven it can run a clean one-van operation, adding AI layers creates confusion, not leverage.
AI should NOT be used to generate fake reviews, fake customer testimonials, or misleading marketing content.

8. Company Doctrine — One Page

THE TWO PRINCIPLES

EARN THE NEXT VISIT
Do honest work. Present findings with evidence. Never optimize for one invoice at the expense of the relationship. Make every completed job increase the probability of the next booked job.
WHY ISN’T SIMON ON A WRENCH?
Simon’s wrench time is the most valuable production asset in the company. Every non-wrench minute must be justified, delegated, automated, or eliminated. The admin partner’s job exists to protect Simon’s wrench time. Every system exists to protect it.

THE DECISION RULE

Every non-wrench task must be one of:
KEEP WITH SIMON  •  DELEGATE  •  AUTOMATE  •  ELIMINATE  •  BUNDLE INTO PAID WORK
If it cannot be justified under one of these categories, the business has failed to build the right system.

HOW THE COMPANY SCALES

Trust earns the next visit. Protected wrench time earns the next dollar.
The company scales by protecting wrench time, not by asking Simon to work harder.
Every system, every hire, every automation exists to add a billable wrench-hour
or remove a non-wrench minute.
